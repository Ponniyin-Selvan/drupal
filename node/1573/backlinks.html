Sudharsanam
A Centre for Arts and Culture
Pudukkottai, Tamilnadu

Collaborative Effort for Preserving Monuments and Enhancing Awareness of our
Heritage
A Proposal for Sittannavasal Monument Complex
(A Conceptual Paper Written in November, 2003)

INTRODUCTION
We have a duty to preserve our glorious heritage. For want of resources and
due to carelessness, many of our precious monuments are in a state of
extreme disrepair. Fortunately, there is a greater awareness among certain
section of the present generation towards the importance of preservation of
our heritage.
Though it is the duty of the government, it must be admitted that
preservation cannot be left to the government alone, and it is the duty of
the public also to shoulder the responsibility.
Some of the concerned citizens of Pudukkottai have been discussing possible
action in this regard. An approach on the following lines may be considered
for the preservation, maintenance and popularisation of Sittannavasal
monuments, to start with. The proposal involves participation of people of
Pudukkottai through Sudharsanam (see Annexure-1 for a detailed note on
Sudharsanam, a centre for arts and culture) and of the Jain community.
Pudukkottai is very often called as an architectural paradise. Three sites
of international repute that lie in this district are Sittannavasal, famous
for the paintings that the Jain cave temple contains and Kodumbalur, for the
exquisite temples and Narttamalai famous for Vijayalaya Cholisvaram temple.
Sittannavasal has a very important place in Indian culture and Tamil
heritage.
The district has the highest number of centrally protected Jaina
relics/monuments in the Tamilnadu. Of the 42 Jaina monuments in the state 30
are in the district of Pudukkottai. (A list of Jaina relics/monuments in
Pudukkottai is given in Annexure-2). All these show that Pudukkottai had a
great Jaina tradition. And probably Sittannavasal was one of the most
important Jaina centre in Tamilnadu, in the past.

MONUMENTS OF SITTANNAVASAL
There are four important sites in and around a large hillock about 200 feet
in height.
1. Arivar-Koil (temple-of-the-Arhat), a Jain cave temple dating to a period
earlier than the 9th century AD and famous for the mural paintings that are
found on its walls.
2. Ezhadi-p-pattam, a place of great sanctity and antiquity for Jain
ascetics practised penance on the polished stone-beds from 3rd century BC
for a thousand years. It has the unique distinction of housing Tamil
inscriptions from the 3rd century BC till the 10th century AD.
3. Navach-chunai, that contains a submerged cave temple.
4. Hundreds of megalithic burial sites, found strewn over.
Of these the first two, namely, the Arivar-koil and the Ezhadi-p-pattam are
associated with the Jaina tradition.

STATE OF PRESERVATION
Most of the monuments cited above are in extreme state of decay. The cave
temple, though intact, the priceless paintings are in a sorry state. The
nature has taken its toll in the last 1200 years. But most damage was
wrought in the last 50 years or so. Once cave temple was decorated with
magnificent paintings over every inch of the space barring the floor. Less
than 10% survive today, mainly due to vandalism and lack of care in the
recent past.
The stone-beds in Ezhadi-p-pattam had 'Mauryan-polish'. But today they stand
vandalised with scratches and doodle. The important inscriptions stand
mutilated. The damage is now arrested by the Many of the megalithic burial sites have crumbled due to weathering action
and of what remains have been encroached upon and destroyed.

PROPOSAL FOR PRESERVATION
The proposal envisages building a HERITAGE COMPLEX in the vicinity of the
Sittannavasal monuments. Specifically it proposes building of
i. a replica of the Arivar-koil, the Jain cave temple, restored to its
original glory with its magnificent mural paintings recreated
ii. four pavilions, highlighting four essential aspects of the monuments
that have bearing on Tamil culture. Thus, we may have four pavilions:
? a painting pavilion to focus on the Indian painting tradition,
? an epigraphy pavilion for showing development of Tamil, other Indian
scripts and scripts of many South-east Asian languages from Brahmi,
? a temple architecture pavilion to trace the development of temple
architecture from cave temple, and, finally,
? a pavilion bringing out the Jain contribution to the Indian heritage.
A plot of sufficient area to accommodate the above and other public utility,
like public conveniences and incorporating a large open house the complex.
The Jain community may build the entire complex and Sudharsanam may run the
complex. We may also seek financial contributions from other individuals.
In this effort the services of a team of experts in the fields, which are on
focus, would be required. Dr. Nagaswamy may be requested to lead the team. A
renowned architect may be engaged in the design of the complex.
A corpus maintained through donation from the public and a reasonable gate
collection may be adequate to maintain the complex.
Such a project executed would help the public to get a glimpse of our lost
heritage of Sittannavasal and provide an 'informed' view of certain aspects
of our culture. Entry to the cave temple proper may, then, be restricted to
scholars enabling the An attempt on these lines would give us experience in public participation
in maintaining our heritage, paving way for attempting similar complexes in
Kodumbalur, Narttamalai, Kudumianmalai, Avudaiyar-koil and Thirumayam, each
one highlighting one or more aspects of Tamil culture.
If the above idea is considered to have any merit, a small group may be
formed to workout the details and prepare a detailed project proposal.
