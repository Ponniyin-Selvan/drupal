****************************************
Rajendra Cholza's naval campaign involved four places
which are at present in Thailand.
Coming to Sukho 'Tai......
Tai is the name of the people.
The 'Tai people originated from Southern China. They were
forced to migrate into the outskirts of the earstwhile Khmer
Cambodian
Empire. They formed a kingdom after a few generations.
This kingdom was known as 'Sukho Tai' - 'The Happiness of
the
Tai people'. At one time it was larger than the present Thailand.
It was replaced by Ayuththaya kingdom.
Later on, the Chakri Dynasty took over. King Mongkut and
his son Chulalongkorn took the kingdom to modern times.
*****************************************

Located approximately 85 km north of Bangkok, the city was the Thai
capital from 1350 to 1767. During this period Thai culture
flourished, and Ayuthaya became a centre for international commerce.
The present day city is located at the confluence of the Chao Phaya,
Pa Sak and Lopburi rivers. A canal joins them and encircles the
town. Ayuthaya has many historic ruins, two museums and is a
fascinating place to visit.

Vanakkam!

My cousin, Balaji sent me this mail today on Angkorwat
based on his personal visit and experience.

I have copies and pasted his mail to me for benefit of
the group members.

Regards,

Uma

Well, it was built around 750AD. But you can find such
mundane details anywhere on the web, such as:
www.angkorwat.org

Things that you should know before you go there (that
may not readily be available elsewhere):

1) The temples are a marvellous manifestation of the
power and religion of an ancient kingdom (Khmer).
Angkor Wat was the power center of this kingdom and
they made every attempt to exhibit this. It is a vast
temple complex. Make sure you have at least three days
to visit the sites. Preferrably five days. The fee is
not small. It is approximately U.S$50 or more
depending on how many days you want to visit.

2) The temples are not as well preserved as in South
India. Remember they were built 1250 years ago but
subsequent wars have permanently razed, buried or
destroyed a large part of this. Whatever remains is
still a wonder, but remember that archeologists are
still trying to understand (and uncover) how vast this
temple complex is.

3) Remember to visit a separate site (~ 50km from the
main Angkor site) called "Bantey Srei". It is a very
small temple but my favorite one and has some of the
most intricately carved temple architecture. Legend
has it that a King's teacher decided go leave his his
power and position, and go do something he loved. So
he left, taking some disciples with him and the result
of his artistic excellence is the temple of Bantey
Srei. Perfectly preserved from the civil war thanks to
its secluded settings, Bantey Srei rivals some of its
counterparts in Tamil Nadu when it comes to intricate
stone carvings. Ramayana, Mahabharata, Krishna's life
depictions ... you can find them all there. Sunset is
specially a nice time to visit Bantey Srei - the
stones get a beautiful reddish hue at sunset and gives
the temple a surreal look of redness.

3) The entire nation of Cambodia has virtually no
paved roads, only dirt "kachcha" roads, with lots of
potholes. Civil war and neglect is just starting to
get repaired. Now they may have some small sections of
tar roads in and around the city of Siem Reap - which
is where Angkor Wat is located. Everywhere else you
have to be prepared for slow, bumpy rides. I went
around much of the country on my own and using my own
travel method. So I know this the hard way. So my
advice is, if you don't have any compelling reasons to
explore the country outside of the Angkor Wat area,
try not to. If you do decide to venture outside Angkor
Wat area, be prepared for some bumpy and dusty rides.

4) Summer is too hot. Winter is the best time to go.
But winter also means you will have many more tourists
visiting the sites, but that's not really a problem.

5) Cambodian people (ethically called "Khmers") are
generally very gentle and polite people, very
hospitable to foreigners. They are also an interesting
mix of Indian and Asian cultural influences. However,
the country is still cleaning up its wounds after a
civil war and genocide. This is the most important
thing you need to be sensitive about. The people are
still piecing together their broken country. It has
been less than 10 years since a peace pact was
signed with the Khmer Rouge. No need to panic though,
things seem peaceful and western tourists have
returned in large numbers (Angkor Wat is on the UN
heritage list and gets lots of UN money and
protection). But it is best to keep this in mind.
Nowhere is it more evident than in the Thai-Cambodia
border town of Poipet. Poipet may well be your
realistic entry point into Cambodia (from Thailand).
This is a largely rundown, lawless town and of no
interest to the tourists. Try to go past the border
town of Poipet as soon as you can. It is a border
checkpost so unfortunately you will have to stop to
get your documents verified. But after that, it is
best to just get into your bus/van and get the hell
out of there. No need to panic though. Just remember
that you are in an unfamiliar country with an unknown
language. Poipet is certainly not a good place to
explore or play the "idhu namma pettai" type game.

6) How to get there:
The bestand most economic way for your Yahoo group to
visit these temples is to fly into Thailand (usually
Bangkok) and then take/charter a bus or van to go
directly to Angkor Wat. I don't exactly remember how
long it took me, but it takes roughly 6-10hrs to go
from Bangkok to the Thai-Cambodian border and another
3-6 hrs further into Cambodia to reach Siem Reap -
where Angkor Wat is. Check some book, website or a
travel agent for exact times. whatever method you
choose, it is best to go directly from Bangkok to Siem
Reap. For this, you need to get your visa ahead of
time, probably in the Cambodia Consulate in India.
However, if you are lucky enough to find an economical
flight from India directly to Siem Reap, then you can
get your visa stamp upon arrival in the airport, with
minimal fuss or delay. However, please confirm this
with the Embassy.

7) If you can, buy a picture book (some French and
American authors are famous for their insights). Look
at the photos, acrhitecture and read about them.
You'll be surprised how closely they resemble South
Indian - style stone carvings.

8) In the main Angkor temple, one of my personal
highlights was to learn about the three "layers" of
the temple. The central temple was surrounded by three
outer layers of protective and ornate stone -cared
walls, connected by stone walkways. the length of the
walkway in each stage corresponded to one particular
'yuga'. Entering each of the three levels was
tantamount to entering the three yugas, before
ultimately reaching heaven (i.e. the main temple built
for Vishnu). Standing there in person and
comprehending the 'yuga' concept was an outstanding
experience, something I have not seen or experienced
in Indian temples (at least based on my
not-so-extensive knowledge of Indian temples). So,
buying a book and reading it certainly has its
benefits.

I bought a DVD there that has some of the local folk
songs and dances. They're quite close to some Indian
forms of Indian songs and dances. But what amused me
most is that, when men and women danced with each
other, men would have the same graceful "mudra" type
dance style that is so typical of femininity in Indian
dances. All those girly Cambodian men !

I wish I had put up my own pictures online, but that
has not yet happened. In the meantime, take a look at
some pictures online:
http://www.terragalleria.com/theravada/cambodia/angkor-wat/angkor-wat.html

That's it, my Mahabharata ends here. hope that gave
you a general idea. If you have any other questions,
feel free to ask.

Balaji
