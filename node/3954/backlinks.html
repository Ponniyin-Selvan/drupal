There was a Seminar on Pandyan History held by the Ancient History
Department of Madurai University in March, 1973. It was the early days of
Madurai University and was known as Madurai University.

I submitted a paper with title, 'Some Feudatories of North Pandya
Country'.
In that paper, I had dealt in detail with the VELirs.
I had started with VEL Pari.
Local legends, geographical features, archeological evidences,
epigraphy, and literary evidences were corroborated and presented.
The session was chaired by Prof. A.S GnyanaSambandhan.

The fore-most historians of the day, Dr.Balakrishnan Nair and
Dr.K.K.Pillai were present at the session. Prof. Karashima of Japan
presented a paper on Pandya Taxation System.

Here is the proof of the Seminar and my participation in it -

http://www.geocities.com/jaybee4127/madurai_university_seminar_1973.html

After the reading of my paper, there was much discussion.
Dr.Nair complimented my paper and gave further clues for
Persian and Arabian connections. He was an authority on Harappa at that
time. He even drew some parallels with Harappa.

This is one venue which has not been sufficiently explored.

Regards

JayBee

Dear Mr.Bala Pillai,

Coincidentally, this topic has also been dealt
with in one my papers.
It was the " Some Feudatories of the North Paandi
Mandalam". This was presented in 1971 in the Seminar on
Paandiyan History in the Madurai University. (At that time
it was just the Madurai University).
Several VeLir chieftains were ruling in that
territory. So I had to deal with the subject of their origin
and their ramifications.
There are very strong indicators connecting them to
Dwaraka.

----------- cut

Puram. 201 states: " Descended from 'fortynine generations' from the
initial origin from the pitcher of a northern sage, Agastya, the vELir
chieftains had ruled over Tuvarai (Dwaraka) of the 'soaring bronze
walls'
". Forty nine generations may be approximately equal to 1000 years.

Actually the sage agasthya is not mentioned by name.
Before that let me give you some back-grounds to the
poem in question.

The VeL Paari was the ruler of Parambu Naadu. He was a scion
of the ancient line of VeL Evvi, the ruler of Muththuuru.
He had two daughters. His best friend was the Brahmin poet,
Kapilar. But Paari was killed by three kings of the victorious
drums who took away his mountain of Parambu.
Kapilar took the girls in his custody and brought them to
Irungo VeL, and asks him to marry them, comparing their
illustrious
lineage.
