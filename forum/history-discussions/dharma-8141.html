Dharma is not impractical. The very word and its roots imply "that which sustains". In the earliest Vedas Dharma was known as 'rta' - that which changes as per cycles. I think the Hindi word 'rithu' is from the same root word.

Dharma is not a monolithic or rigid concept. It changes per place, time andperson. It is just not a philosophical term and neither Absolute.

We Indians are diverse and it would be wrong to expect us to act like a homogeneous entity in all our customs and traditions. The recent the present generation/days we don't feel kinship is because we have cut our chords to our rich historical past that gave us dharma. Instead of people belonging to'Dharma' we have been shone off the great uniting concept. Now we ourselves as a madrasi guy or a hindi girl etc.

I disagree that we are as strange to say a guy from UP as we are strange toa guy from USA. With the UP guy, we share history, we share culture, tradition, dharma that we do not share with the person from USA.

It is indeed sad that we are losing valuable insights on Dharma. We are ourselves to blame.

The lady hygienist in the dentist's office ushered me to a reclining chair and said that I should wait for the dentist to come. In that natural posture of relaxation I closed my eyes and almost took a nap when a gentle voice woke me up from behind. "I'm Faz and how are you Tim?" He must have read my name from my file I thought. I noticed a labored American accent. Not able to see the face, I was trying to make a guess as to the strange first name's nationality. Was the name 'Fuzz' or 'Fas' I debated but in this country of immigrants it could belong to any nationality, I thought, not failing to note the familiarity in his accent.  He continued his talk with thehygienist about the X-rays. I was getting closer and closer to his accent before I said to myself "Munda punjabi di?" (Is the boy from Punjab?)

He smiled and said, "I'm from Pakistan and when I saw your last name I knew you are from India." To that, I replied when I heard his first name I thought hewas from Ukraine. "Fazal Ahmed," he clarified and laughed at the possibility of being mistaken for a Ukrainian. At last it made sense. We both realized we had camouflaged our identities with our respective adopted first names.
 
He is from Lahore, the cultural capital of Pakistan. I pointed out to himthat the ritzy Punjabis on both sides of the border speak in a similar inflection. He was amused with my observation. He had heard of the phrase "India is shining" and wanted to know if that was true. I stayed politically correct and talked about outsourcing, software exports and the changes all around. He said he would like to visit India some day.
 
After some more small talk, the inevitable subject surfaced, "I'm surprised India lost to Pakistan by 341 runs in the final test at Karachi," he said; his voice could not hide some of his excitement. I said our best batsmencollapse under pressure and he acknowledged that Pakistanis were no different. He was sad that Ganguly was not treated properly and wondered if Sachin Tendulkar's days as a dependable batsman were over. I told him that the one day series would go in India's favor to which he replied he hoped so butwas sure that Pakistan would win. The last sentence he spoke in Urdu. I replied in Hindi that changes were inevitable.
 
We talked about the good old days of cricket ??" Sunil Gavaskar, Javed Miandad, and the legendary Hanif Mohammad. We even wistfully talked abouthow formidable a combined team of our two nations would be.
When I left the clinic, I felt my cricket repertoire was fully refurbished. In Red Sox nation where one can't be anything but a baseball fan, only ahandful can talk about cricket as passionately.
 
Weeks later the 'oneday limited overs' matches went in India's favor. The results were amazing. Indian cricketer, Mahendra Singh Dhoni, became the heart throb of millions of Pakistanis. Pakistani President Pervez Musharraf openly praised the young Indian team's talent. There was magic in the air when the teams played and Indians won repeatedly in four matches, the only occasion when they lost was on a technical formula. Even the lay Pakistani person seemed to enjoy the Indian victory.
 
Soon enough, I planned to go and meet Faz and swagger about my prophecieshaving come true. I knew he would agree with me and say something in his distinct Punjabi accent.
 
Except on cricket grounds, hockey fields, the Line of Control and Siachenglacier, Indians and Pakistanis seem to get along just fine.
