Hi Gandhi, the context of 'asocial' as pertaining to the nobel prize
is the inability for an external entity to judge the impact of the
author's work on society. The readers of RKN's books were restricted
to upper middle class/rich elite well read people although he wrote
about the common man. If it were today he would have online fans or
other means where one can judge the readers views and how he goes
down with audience. In those days there were none. BTW as Kathie
pointed out some english critics did consider his english awkward,
although that is debatable. More indian critics like Shashi Tharoor
thought he was story book like, no serious comment on culture/society
and anything for adults. (These are not my opinions, BTW).

Malathi
re: during one of his interviews in his 90th birthday he said his

purpose was to write stories. he said that is his objective. he liked
people reading books. reading books is a very good habit and therefore
he helps society. he wanted to be known as a storyteller. he compared
himself to the village brahmin who gives "kathakalatshebam" during the
sivarathri times. he said he likes villages but wanted to live in a
city. he said he liked chennai the most because it is both trditional
and modern.

his stories and characters depict the detchment a hindu tries to
achieve in his life. for example novels like vendor of sweets. he
depicted the spontaneous evolution of an indian yogi in "guide"- a fact
conveniently missed in the movie version.

his humor has certain undercurrent tragedies most of the time.
financial expert and mr.sampath for example. some of his characters
like daisy in painter of signs was excellant.

chennai had his admirers like HINDU -RAM. his works were published till
a few months prior to his death. the loss of his daughter to cancer had
very much affected him in his final years.

he is relevant in modern society. i think story tellers are not yet a
vanished tribe-even in the era of net and cellphones.

gandhi
