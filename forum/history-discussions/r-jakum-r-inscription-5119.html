The R?jakum?r Inscription

Background
The Rajakumar Inscriptions are inscribed on two stone pillars,
designated A and B by C.O. Blagden who started work on the
transcription and translation.
Pillar A, now located in the Bagan Museum, was found by Emil
Forchhammer, Professor of Pali at Rangoon College and Government
Archaeologist, in 1886-87, soon after the British annexation of
Upper Myanmar. It was found intact north of the Myazedi Pagoda and
quite close to it, in front of a small ruined temple.
Pillar B, now located on the platform of the Myazedi Pagoda to its
southeast, was in four pieces when first found. Two pieces of the
upper half were found in the Gubyaukgyi Temple in 1886-87 at the
same time as Pillar A. The 3rd piece of the upper half was found in
1904 in the enclosure wall of the Myazedi Pagoda into which it had
been immured. The 4th piece, the lower half, was found accidentally
buried in a small brick building east of the Myazedi.
Pillar A, the smaller of the two, is 1.4 m high, with each of its
four faces 35.6 cm wide. Pillar B, less regular, is 2.2 m high; the
two broader faces are 52.1 cm wide while the other two faces taper
down from 30.1 cm at the top to 26.7 cm at the base.
Since Pillar A was found intact at the Myazedi Pagoda, the
Inscription was named the Myazedi Inscription. But this was a
misnomer because the Insceiption dated back to the 11th century
while the Myazedi was built in the late 19th century by villagers.
The Inscription made reference to a cave-pagoda and this is
identified with the Gubyaukgyi which adjoins the Myazedi in the
west, and the Inscription is now referred to by its author,
Rajakumar, the donor of the Gubyaukgyi.
Texts
The Inscription is in four languages -- Myanmar, Mon, Pyu and Pali --
with each text inscribed on each of the four faces. The Myanmar,
Mon and Pali texts are written in a script derived from the Pallava
script of southern India and used by the Mons of the Dvaravati
kingdom in Thailand from about the 6th century. The earliest
representatives of the script in Myanmar are the Tr?p and Pandit
Inscriptions found at Thaton. In Bagan, the script was earlier used
by Anawrahta (Sr? Aniruddhadeva, 1044?-1077?) in the Pali
inscriptions on his votive tablets.
The Pyu text is written in another script, one derived from the
Kadamba script of southern India, early representatives of the
script being found at Sriksetra, including a 5th century gold-leaf
manuscript of excerpts from the Pali canon. In Bagan the script as
well as the Pyu language was used in an inscription on a votive
tablet of Sr? Ba??no attributed to the reign of Anawrahta.
The texts on Pillars A and B are identical although there are some
variations in spelling. On Pillar A, the Myanmar and Pyu texts are
on opposite faces; on Pillar B, the Myanmar and Pali texts are on
the opposite broader faces. Since the pillars differ in size, the
texts differ in the number of lines on each pillar:

The Pali text is different from the others in that it is written in
verse (g?th?), while the other three texts are in prose The Myanamr
text is the earliest known Myanmar inscription, being preceded only
by a short piece of Myanmar writing imbedded in Kyansittha's Myakan
Inscription which is in Mon. This gives the name of the tank which
Kyansittha dug as "Mahanirbban Lakchuykhiriy (Hand - Drawn - and -
Carried Water). Despite the strong claims of Mon as the original
text - with Kyansittha's inscriptions as well as the ink glosses of
the Jataka paintings of Rajakumar's Gubyaukgyi being in Mon -
scholars are of the opinion that the syntax in such sentences in the
Mon text as "in the city of Arimaddanapur King
Tribhuvanadityadhammaraja was king", "one queen of that king was
named Trilokavatamsakadevi", "that queen's son was named Rajakumar"
which conform to the Myanmar syntax of having the verb at the end
indicate that the original text was Myanmar and that it was from
Myanmar that translations were made.
Translations
An English translation of the Myanmar text by U Tun Nyein,
Government Translator, was published in 1899 and a French
translation of the Pali text was published by Leon de Beylie in his
Prome et Samara in 1907. An improved English translation of both was
made by Charles Duroiselle of the Burma Archaeological Survey in
1919 (Epigraphia Birmanica, II, i).
The Pyu and Mon texts were much more difficult than the Myanmar and
Pali texts and when they were sent to C.O. Blagden of the School of
Oriental Studies in London for study he expressed the difficulties
and his own inadequacies:
"The ink-impressions supplied to me were often imperfect
representations of much weathered and battered originals, which of
course I have never had an opportunity of seeing .... To do full
justice to these records would require in the editor a number of
qualifications that are seldom found united; amongst others, a
thorough knowledge of Mon, Sanskrit and Pali, much experience in
Indian epigraphy, great familiarity with Buddhist thought and
legend, an intimate acquaintance with local conditions of various
parts of Burma, much leisure and a good deal of patience. To these,
except the last two, I can lay no claim."
The Pyu language was completely unknown when Blagden made his
attempt and it was he who identified the unknown text of the
Rajakumar Inscription as Pyu. It was only the existence of parallel
texts which enabled Blagden to attempt a translation, and although
he was able to make positive identification of only some of the
words, with other identifications provisional, he accomplished an
English translation of the Pyu text in 1911 (Journal of the Royal
Asiatic Society, 1911).
For his work on the Mon text Blagden had for reference the 2nd
edition of J.M. Haswell's Grammatical Notes and Vocabulary of the
Peguan Language (1901), but the work was still difficult:
"The early stage of the [Mon] language was quite unknown to
everybody: even an intimate acquaintance with modern Mon, had I
possessed it, would not have made it at once intelligible. The
changes wrought by upward of eight hundred years are often so
radical that it is very difficult to recognize quite common words,
e.g. one may be excused for not seeing at a glance that ji?jun is
the same as the modern dayuin or that kandar is the old form of
kalaw."
Nevertheless, Blagden completed an English translation of the Mon
text in 1909, (Journal of the Royal Asiatic Society, 1909) revised
it in 1910 (Journal of the Royal Asiatic Society, 1910) and again in
1919 (Epigraphia Birmanica II, i).
Significance
All four texts of the Rajakumar Inscription were of significance for
historical and linguistic studies.
The immediate significance of the Inscription for historical studies
was the fixing of the regnal dates of the king referred to in the
Inscription as Sri Tribhuvan?ditya-dhammar?ja, identified with
Kyansittha of the chronicles. In the three major chronicles
Kyansittha's regnal dates appear as:

In a number of inscriptions by Kyansittha himself (Myakan,
Alanpagan, Shwesandaw, Kyaikte, Kyaiktalan), the date of his
accession was given as Sas 1630 (AD 1086/7) which C.O. Blagden
attempted to explain thus: "This may be a statement in round numbers
or may possibly refer to the date of his actual anointment as king".
Later historians have not offered any other explanation, and such
historians as Than Tun ("History of Burma AD 1000-1300," Bulletin of
the Burma Historical Commission, I, i, June 1960) merely note. "In
AD 1084 Thiluin Man (Kyansittha) became king but his coronation was
held possibly two years later."
The significance of the date of Kyansittha's accession was first
noted in 1917 ("Dates of King Kyansittha's accession and
coronation", Annual Report of the Archaeological Survey of Burma,
1917) and the door was opened for a revision of the king-list and
regnal dates of the chronicles on the basis of the inscriptions. A
major step was taken by Ma Mya Than in 1932 ("Some of the Earlier
Kings of Pagan Dynasty", Journal of the Burma Research Society XXII,
ii) but not all the issues have been settled (see Tilman
Frasch, "The Mt. Thetso Inscription Re-examined," Myanmar Historical
Research Journal 2, Jun 1999).
In the chronicles there is no mention at all of a Rajakumar. The
information they provide on Kyansittha's offspring are represented
in the New Chronicle account:
o "The queens of Htilaing Min [Kyansittha] were: Apeyadana;
Khin Ohn, the princess of Ussa [Pegu]; Khin Tan, daughter of the
headman of Htilaing; and Thambula, village maiden of Kyaungbyu and
niece of Mahathera, whom he made queen with the name Uhsaukpan. Of
the four, a daughter Shwe-einthe was born to Apeyadana, there is no
mention in writings of a son or daughter being born to Khin Ohn and
Khin Tan, a son Jeyakhettara was born to Uhsaukpan".

Historians have identified Uhsaukpan, "Jewelled Head Ornament," of
the chronicles with the Trilokavatamsakadevi, "Chaplet of the Three
Worlds", of the Inscription and Jeyakhettara of the chronicles with
Rajakumar. However, the only information provided by the chronicles
on Jeyakhettara -- apart from the circumstances of his conception
and birth in Kyaungbyu and his arrival at the court with his mother -
- is that he was made ruler of Dha??avat? (Arakan) and the Seven
Districts of the Western Range.
A votive tablet was found at Bagan in 1931-32 in a ruined temple
southeast of the Nagayon Temple which bore the legend in Pali in Mon-
Myanmar script:
o eso bhagav? Trilokavatamsak?mah?dev?

o ya kato vimuttattham sahatthenevati

o This Buddha Trilokavatamsakamahadevi

o to be emancipated made with her hands.

Although the title is slightly different and although the chronicles
mention another Uhsaukpan -- the queen of Kyansittha's predecessor
Sawlu -- the votive tablet has been attributed to
Tritokavatamsakadevi of the Inscription.
Beyond initiating a study of the inscriptions to revise the
chronicles, the Rajakumar Inscription had a much wider significance.
As the earliest Myanmar text, it provided valuable material for the
study of the Myanmar language of the early 12th century. The Pali
text, too, written in verse, helped to provide an indication of the
mastery of Pali language at Bagan in the early 12th century. But the
greatest contribution of the Inscription was to the study of Pyu and
Mon texts.
Following his success with the Rajakumar Inscription, Blagden was
provided with some more Pyu inscriptions. Some of these (the Bebe
Pagoda Inscription, the Kyaukka Thein Inscription, an inscription
found at Bagan whose reverse face was in Chinese) were much defaced
and could not be deciphered. But Blagden made an English translation
in 1914 (Epigraphia Indica, XIII) of four short inscriptions on
funerary urns found near the Payagyi Pagoda at Sriksetra. The
inscriptions provided three names of kings of a Vikrama dynasty
which reigned at Sriksetra, presumably in the late 7th and early 8th
century, and enabled Blagden to make a conjectural identification of
the Pyu numerals.
Other brief inscriptions found at Sriksetra indicated a Varman
dynasty contemporaneous with the Vikrama. Thus the knowledge of the
Pyu language provided by the Rajakumar Inscription made it possible
to extract what information there was in the scanty Pyu texts.
Much greater advances were made in Mon where there was a greater
wealth of texts. Blagden followed up his translation of the Mon text
with translations of Kyansittha's Mon inscriptions--the Great
Shwezigon Inscription, Myakan Inscription, Alanpagan Inscription,
Shwesandaw Inscription (Epigraphia Birmanica, II, ii), and the
Palace Inscription (Epigraphia Birmanica, III)--with the Great
Shwezigon Inscription (8 faces) and the Palace Inscription (12
faces) providing a wealth of detail on the ideology and ceremony of
the Court of Bagan in the late 11th century.
Blagden's translations stimulated the study of the Mon inscriptions
by George Coedes, Curator of the National Library of Bangkok
(Bulletin de I'Ecole Francaise d'Extreme Orient, XXV). The earliest
of these inscriptions were the four inscriptions on an octagonal
pillar found in a ruined temple in Lop Buri and placed in the
National Museum. Coedes sent an ink-impression to Duroiselle in
Yangon and a photograph to Blagden in London and, with some help
from them, made his translation. They were short inscriptions
(11,7,4,3, lines) recording various donations and were undated but
Coedes placed them in the 8th century on the basis of the form of
the letters.
The other inscriptions were from monasteries in Lamphun (Wat Don,
Wat Kukut, Wat Sen Khao Ho). The inscriptions from Wat Don (Pali 7?
lines, Mon 28? lines) and Wat Kukut (Pali 6? lines, Mon 30 lines)
provided the name Sabb?dhisiddhi - a king who was also mentioned in
the Pali chronicle Jinak?lam?lini written in 1516 - and three dates
which Coedes identified as being between AD 1213 and AD 1220. These
provided the basis for Coedes to attempt a re-working of the
chronology of the Jinak?lam?lini and in particular to establish the
date of accession of ?dityaraj?, Sabbadhisiddhi's ancestor, as ca
1150 and to date the outbreak of the epidemic at Haripu?jaya
(Lamphun) which led to its inhabitants fleeing to Sudhammapura
(Thaton) a century earlier in the middle of the 11th century.
TheRajakumar Inscription thus unlocked many doors and opened up many
avenues in historical and linguistic studies.
Appendix I: Translations
Myanmar Text (A 39 lines, B 34, lines)
Prosperity! Honour to the Buddha! One thousand six hundred and
twenty-eight years of the Buddha's religion having elapsed, Sri
Tribhuvanadity?dhammar?j become king in this city of Arimaddanapur.
A beloved wife of that king was named Trilokavatamsak?dev?, and that
beloved wife's son was named R?jakum?r. That king gave the beloved
wife three villages of slaves. That beloved wife having died, the
king gave her ornaments and the three villages of slaves to her son
named R?jakum?r. At the time that that king having reigned twenty-
eight years, became sick even unto death that son of the beloved
wife, who was named R?jakum?r, calling to mind the favours of the
great king who had nurtured him, made an image of the Buddha all in
gold. At the time he offered (it) he spoke thus: "I, your slave,
made this golden Buddha for my lord; the three villages of slaves
which my lord gave me, I gave to this golden Buddha." At that time
the king, being delighted, said: "Good, Good!" and, in the presence
of the great monk Mahather, the great monk Muggaliputtatissatther,
the great monk Sumedha the learned, the great monk Brahmapal, the
great monk Brahmadiv, the great monk Son and the great monk
Sanghasena the very learned, the king poured water. That being done,
that son of the beloved wife who was named R?jakum?r enshrined that
golden Buddha, and made this cave-pagoda with a golden spire. Having
finished (it), at the time he dedicated this cave-pagoda and Buddha,
he brought up (the folk of) Sakmunalon, one village, Rap?y, one
village, Henbuiw, one village, these three villages of slaves. That
son of the beloved wife, who was named R?jakum?r, poured water for
this cave-pagoda and Buddha, and thus said: "Let this deed of mine
be the cause of my obtaining Omniscience! After me, (whosoever)
whether (he be) my son, or grandson, or relative, or any other
person, if he oppresses the slaves I have offered to this Buddha,
may he not behold the Buddha Arimittiy?!"
Mon Text (A 33 lines, B 46 lines)

Lord Buddha had gone on for one thousand six hundred and twenty-
eight years, in this city of Arimaddanapur King Sri

named Trilokavatamsakadevi; that queen's son was named R?jakum?r.
The king gave three villages of slaves to the queen. When the queen
died, the king gave all the queen's goods, together with the three

After the king had reigned for twenty-eight years, when the king was

Then the king being well pleased, expressed approval,

the learned Sumedha, the lord teacher Brahmapal, the lord teacher

Pali Text (A 41 lines, B 40, lines)

Prosperity! Having bowed to the Buddha and other (two) Excellent
Objects, I shall relate the noble work of merit performed, in the
Conqueror's dispensation, by R?jakum?ra. Hearken to me! When one
thousand six hundred and twenty-eight years had elapsed after the
Nibbana of the Lord of the World, Tibhuvanadicca, a powerful king
and scion of the exalted solar race, became king in the city called
Arimaddana. He had a beloved queen, Tilokavatamsik? (by name), who
was benevolent and skilful in all the affairs of the king. She had a
son, R?jakum?ra by name, a minister, zealous in (the management of)
the affairs of state, prudent and wise. The king, graciously
disposed towards the queen, gave her three villages for her to
enjoy, for life, the revenues (accruing from) the slaves (thereof).
Now, upon the death of the queen, the king again made those three
villages over to R?jakum?ra. On the king, after having ruled
righteously for twenty-eight years, being seized with a mortal
illness he (R?jakum?ra), remembering the numerous and great favours
of the righteous king, had a beautiful image of the Buddha made all
in gold and, carrying it with great pomp, and well pleased (with his
meritorious act), approached unto the presence of the king and told
him of his intentions:
o "On your Majesty's behalf, I have made this beautiful
golden image of the Master; deign, by lord, to rejoice in this
excellent work of merit! The three villages which you formerly gave
me, I have dedicated to this image; at this also rejoice!"

On this being said, the king, (though) his mind was troubled by
disease, rejoiced and, pleased and delighted, exclaimed; "Good!
good!" The compassionate Mahathera, the thera Muggaliputtaka,
Sumedha the wise, so called on account of his wisdom, Brahmapala,
likewise Brahmadeva the virtuous, Sona, the learned and eminent
scholar called Sanghasena:, in the presence of these monks (the
king), well pleased, poured the libation on the ground, taking the
earth to witness. Then, the minister enshrined the beautiful golden
image and built (over it) a cave-pagoda surmounted by a golden
spire. Having performed the dedication ceremony for the Buddha-image
and the cave-pagoda, he (R?jakum?ra), weary of repeated birth, made
the following aspiration:
? May this meritorious work, performed and accumulated by
me, be the cause of my attaining Ommicience! Whosoever, be he my
son, or grandson, or any other relative, with evil intent and
unbelieving mind should oppress those slaves, dwellers in the three
villages, whom I have dedicated to the cave-pagoda and the golden
image of the Great Sage, may this vile person be precluded from
beholding Metteyya, the Chief of men!"
