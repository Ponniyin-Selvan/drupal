Subject:saiva nayanmars 63? 9?

Saiva concept of nayanmars - a curious question
The saiva nayanmars are 63 in number. Tokaiyatiyar are
another 9. Surprisingly, Manickavasakar is not one of
the first 63 which is explained with reference to the
fact that he belonged to a later period to Sundarar.
There are occasions when one wonders why a few names
had been included in the first list. For example,
Sundarar's parents are included in the list. The
criteria of the list appear to be arbitrary. In fact
the names of nayanmars cannot be confined to the
number 63. Perhaps, the number 63 was tentatively
assumed as a number of significance in line with the
Jain concept of 63 tirttarankaras?. This needs
explanation.

Moreover, thillaival antanar is a name in the list of
the tokaiyatiyar. It is common knowledge that they
were 3000 in number. So the number 9 is arbitrary in
that it is not a number of actual values. So also
'appalum ati serntar' is explained claiming that it
refers to those who are devoted to Siva and living
outside the geographical area of Tamilnadu. Perhaps,
the reference is not only geographical linearity but
also linearity of time. Perhaps the intention is to
accept any one who fulfils certain criteria in future
and elsewhere to be included in the list of nayanmars.

Whoever wherever lives according to the tenets of
Saivam at any point of time is a nayanmar? could it
mean that? I will be happy if this issue of numbers is
taken for serious discussion.

By comparison is to be seen that the Vaishnavites are
more precise in their list of the alvars. They are
twelve; and to some they are ten. Whatever is the
number, it stands for a particular definite numerical
value unlike in the list of Saivism. Perhaps
explanations and interpretations will be in place in
this context.

From: jaybee Subject:saiva nayanmars 63? 9?

JayBee Wrote:

This question has been dealt from several angles by Saivite
scholars all along.
But it is one of the mysteries on the present day Saivism.
There are several questions that arise pertaining to Saivism.
This happens to be one of them.
Why was MaaNikkavaasagar not included in the ThiruththoNdar
list?
The common answer is that Vaasagar lived later than Sundarar.
Possible.

But the doctrinal contrasts may also be a problem there.
Manikkavaasagar's doctrines could easily clash with
the Agamic Saivism of the later times.
And being a citizen of an enemy country is another nagging
possibilty.
Though Pandya Naadu was bigger and had reached
empire status during the ThiruththoNdar times, still we see only
16 people belonging to the Pandya country among the Nayanmaars.
And the paadal peRRa thalams are only 14. All the three ThEvaaram
authors have visited Pandya Naadu.
You have to compare these numbers with the preponderance
of the number of Thalams in Cholza Naadu/ThoNdai Nadu; and also
the number of Naayanmaars from the Cholza Nadu/ThoNdai Naadu
Axis.
The Saivite canon was finally compiled by the Cholziyans.
Here again is a 'nerudal'.
One reason that could be quoted, Chola was predominantly
Saivite; but Pandya was predominantly Jain; and it was converted
later.
But then, Pallava was also predominantly Jain and got
converted about 35 years earlier. It happened in the childhood of
Sambandhar - he himself says "paal nal vaayan ippaalan,
iinggu ivan enRu eNNI, nii parivu eydhidEl". Thats the first
encounter of Magaiyarkkarasi with Sambandhar. The queen has
her doubts about the childhood state of Sambandhar.

The first person to establish "matams" or monastries was
Appar. His doctrine was "Dhayaa Muula Dhanmam".
Sambandhar established his own matams at least 25 years
later.
His doctrines have sharp contrasts with Appar's.
But at present, we see only Sambandhar matams around.
What happened to all the Naavukkaraiyan Thirumatams?
Thiruth ThoNdaththogaiyaan Thirumatams? The Paasupatha matams?
The KaaLaamukha Saiva matams?
And lastly, the Kaapaalika/Bairava institutions?
Even the Sambandhar matams at the present times are
Meykandar school matams.
All the other schools and doctrines have disappeared.
Even the Dhayaa Muula Dhanmam of the Great Appar is
known only by name.
And this term is known only to a minute, miniscule,
microscopically little number of people.

So there is mysterious gap there.

Regards

JayBee
