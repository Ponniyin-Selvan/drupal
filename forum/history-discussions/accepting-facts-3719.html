Yes. We should accept if any theory is proven wrong with proper
supporting evidences whether it is about not accepting a finding because of insufficient or weak evidences
doesn’t mean that we are not ready to change our opinions. Every
research finding is subject to be proven wrong eventually. Because,
all the conclusions are based on evidences. When we find new
evidences, direction of the conclusion will also change or get
stronger. It should not be considered as the mistake of researcher.
For example, earlier it was believed that co-rule period between
Rajaraja and Rajendra is 2 years based on the inscriptions read till
then. But recent findings proved that it is 3 years. So, all
conclusions based on this 2 years co-rule will also change. It
doesn’t mean that the scholar who found the 2 years co-rule is not a
good researcher or liar. But, what makes people hesitate to accept
the new findings? I see 3 reasons.

1. Taking things personal.

Some scholars take it personal when their finding is not accepted.
They fear that people may start doubting their capability and
credibility if any of their findings is proven wrong. So, they try
to defend their side and don’t correct themselves even if they
understand the fact. They use their social status, background, etc
as weapons and say like 'I am so-and-so person. That is why others
don’t accept my points' or 'I disproved his finding. So, he is doing
the same with me', etc. As long as their research methodology is
proper, don’t have to worry about the criticisms on their
conclusions.

2. Drawing attention

Some scholars create unnecessary controversies to get short term
fame. 2-3 years before, a scholar (don’t want to mention his name)
from Tamil university gave a press report with photos that he found
sculptures of Rajaraja and Rajendra in Udayaloor near Kumbakonam.
When we visited that temple, there were three sculptures under the
feet of Vayir kaavalar. No evidence to identify that they are and feet of a gate keeper? And he didn’t mention anything about the
third person. I don’t know why they don’t understand that people
can easily verify what they are saying and what would be the opinion
about that scholar after verification. Every angry if he reads the news given by him and sees the sculptures. I
think these people say such things because many of us don’t go and
verify the findings and just take what the articles say.

3. Brain washed beliefs

Everyone believes something from the childhood or told by someone we
love and admire so much. We start building a strong belief on that
and look at things in that perspective. When such things are
disproved or questioned, we are not ready to think about it and
start resisting immediately. It is sad that even the educated people
and scholars have such behavior. So many examples can be quoted from
various planes like the davinci code matter, images of the kings and
queens portrayed by kalki, period of vedas and epics, identifying
the character of a person with his caste, sayings of political and
religious leaders, etc. We should try to be broad minded to accept
fact as a fact. But at the same time, we should try to understand
the difference between cross verification and resisting.

As far as I know, all the scholars accepted that Pulikesi is a great
art lover. I don’t see anyone denying it.

Thanks
Kamal

Dear Venkatesh,

Thanks for the comments.

I am not sure if my usages of the word ‘evidence’ sounds like 'only
inscriptions' or any scholar who claims that only inscriptions
should be considered as evidences.

IMHO, inscriptions are not the only evidences. We have to consider
architecture, sculpture, copper plates, literature and of course
common sense. But common sense also sometimes leads to confusion. I
am stating an incidence which i forgot to include in my
article 'Enniyirundhadhu eedera..'.

In Kundrakkudi, we climbed to top of the hill to see brahmi
inscriptions. There we found a row of 'samana padukkai'. Each one
was around 5 to 6 feet length and hardly 1 or 1.5 feet width. We
wondered how a person could sleep in 1.5 feet width bed. Our common
sense didn’t allow us to believe that someone would have slept
there. But, next day when we went to the big temple, we took rest in
the 3rd floor where the people involved in renovation work were also
taking rest. There, we saw a person sleeping on a wooden board whose
length was less than a feet. Then we understood that it would have
been possible for samana saints to sleep in that small rock bed.

So, the level of common sense varies from person to person. It is
the experience which changes the perception and the way we use
common sense. If we have seen the person sleeping in less than a
feet width bed before, we wouldn’t have wondered on that 'samana
padukkai'.

RRC is great for us and his crowning
achievement is the big temple. but during
the same century and the ones preceding
and succeeding it a dozen equivalent
monuments were built in kingdoms of india
with not so much resources as cholas. in
kalinga, in madyapradesh, in bihar.

some of those kings we don’t even know by name.

Reply for this is the last para in my mail on 'I think rrc is great
because...'. It applies to both Rajaraja as well as Big temple.

The last point is, about the available information
on other kings of india. I agree that we should
not underrate a king just because of not having
any record. But only if we have proper evidences,
we can say it as a fact. Otherwise, it would be
mere fiction. Those assumptions cannot come
under history. As of now, we have enough evidences
to prove RRC as 'The Greatest' king of India. The
validity period of this conclusion expires when
another king is proved as 'The Greatest' with
proper evidences.

Thanks
Kamal
