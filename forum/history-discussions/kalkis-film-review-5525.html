http://randorguy.galatta.com/blog/randorguy/Lakshmikantham-
Murde/bdid/309.html

November 8, 1944? Madras, a sea-kissed sunny city founded some 350
plus years ago by an employee of the East India Company, Francis
Day, the provincial capital woke up as usual as the resplendent
sun
rose
out of the blue waters of the Bay of Bengal. To one of its
citizens,named C. N. Lakshmikantham, a colorful character of
considerable notoriety, a popular journalist of the 'yellow'
variety
it would be his last day on this planet. Only he was blissfully
ignorant of it all. Indeed if someone had told him so that day-
break he would have
laughed it off, mixing it with some rich colorful two-letter Tamil
words (the equivalent of the English four-lettered ones!) and most
likely he would have slapped that bold someone! Such was the man,
the
earliest of yellow journalists, the prince of muck-rakers.

After finishing his mundane morning chores this go-getter was on
his
way in a man drawn rickshaw to meet his lawyer an Indian Christian
who looked more like a Shakespearean actor with his wavy hair, long
side-burns and all named J. Nargunam. At that time he had only a
modest practice and lived on Maddox Street, Vepery, not very far
from where Lakshmikantham lived in a narrow house-lined street in
nearby
Purasawalkam. The client and lawyer were good friends and met
almost every evening after court hours.

Lakshmikantham was quite a court-bird. And a big of a jailbird
too.
Indeed his pre-journalist life reads like a chapter from a best
selling suspense thriller. He had ambitions of becoming a lawyer,
something he loved but he could not afford to go to Law College.
Yet he did not forget it and read law books. He frequented law
courts of Madras city and hobnobbed with small fry lawyers,
briefless, breadless
ones and litigants of the illiterate kind. Clever and quick-
witted
he soon became a law tout. Touts though prohibited by law are men
who take cases and other legal matters to lawyers for a substantial
'kick-back' in the fee. Patronised by briefless and struggling
lawyers some touts in Madras with a smattering of legal knowledge
earn more than most lawyers. They draft deeds and documents,
wills
and
notices mostly for illiterates and some touts resort to forgery
and
fabricating documents very casually of course, for a stiff fee.

Lakshmikantham was one such tout and smart enough to argue his own
cases in court. One of his shady deals involving the fabrication
of
a sworn affidavit created by him in a case filed against him by a
leading Madras daily found him in the dock in the Madras High
Court
Original Sessions. During the hearing he escaped hoodwinking a
posse of cops and for a while he lived a happy-go-lucky life,
until
the cops
nabbed and brought him to trial. Found guilty he received a 7
year
jail sentence and was ordered to be imprisoned at Rajamundry, then
in Madras presidency and now in Andhra Pradesh. While the train
taking
him was crossing a river in Andhra he, hand-chained, managed to
escape fooling the guarding cops and jumped from the chug-chugging
train into the river below shrouded by darkness. With chains and
all he swam
across the big river and disappeared into the dark night. He
roamed
around freely for a long while until an angry girl friend tipped
off
the searching Madras city cops. Back in custody he was declared
a 'dangerous convict' and deported to the Andamans. With an ocean
surrounding this famed island prison, Lakshmikantham could not
even
dream of escape. He served his term until as a war security
measure
he along with others was set fee and shipped back home.

Back in Madras, a notorious ex-convict, he avoided courts and cops
for whom he had an understandable allergy! Life had to go on and
he
dabbled in many things. He ran a small restaurant serving meat
dishes
which in south India were then known as "military hotels"
{Eateries
serving only vegetarian food were called 'civil' hotels}. He lost
in this venture and looked around for greener pastures. Soon he
found
one, Journalism. A profession that would bring him money, fame or
more precisely notoriety, and a violent end.

Around 1943 he launched a film weekly in Tamil "Cinema Thoothu".
It
was a different kind of magazine. Lakshmikantham did not publish
articles about theories and trends in cinema and such. He filled
the
pages with tales of bedroom activities, amours and amatory
adventures of prominent filmland figures like reigning superstar
M.
K. Thyagaraja Bhagavathar, brilliant comedian hailed as
the 'Charlie
Chaplin of
India', N. S. Krishnan and his star wife T. A. Mathuram,
successful
producer S. M. Sreeramulu Naidu and many others enjoying stardom
and
immense popularity in south India. He ran "series" about
Bhagavathar and Krishnan as a weekly feature. He wielded a facile
pen and his writing was lively, punchy and enjoyable to read.
Bhagavathar, a singing star was then at his zenith of his career
and
fame. More a singer than an actor his following was incredible.
Masses, classes, intellectuals, upper crust folks, all loved him,
his music. Women
adored him and many society dames threw themselves at him. Such
was
his charisma. At this time he was one of the most famous figures
of
south India, a household name.

No wonder, "Cinema Thoothu", the first of its kind was a raving
sell-
out and copies sold at a premium. The Tamil-speaking world had
never seen anything like this weekly. The south Indian society,
some
forty and odd years ago was conservative, old-fashioned and
subjects
like sex were strictly taboo and barely whispered. In such rigid,
regimented atmosphere not surprisingly people loved gossip and
scandal
and hungered to read and know all about the goings-on behind
closed
doors in high places. Movie glamour had already seeped into the
skin and soul of society and this wily pen-totter took a hundred-
and-
ten
per cent advantage of it all.

What was Lakshmikantham's aim in running such a magazine? He
professed, at least in public to be a traditionalist with a
missionary zeal to uphold the ideals and high values of ancient
Indian culture.
He claimed he was socially conscious reformer come to earth, to
clean the stables free of sinners and seducers, adulterers and the
likes. But his real aim, the sole purpose was blackmail, pure and
simple.
And surely it paid him ample dividends. Many actors, actresses
and
cinema men paid him hush money to buy his silence.

Some like Bhagavathar complained to the authorities and a
memorandum
signed by the superstar and other victims of the blackmailer's
vitriolic pen was submitted to Sir Arthur Hope the then Governor of
Madras. At once he acted and the license for publication was
cancelled. However Lakshmikantham was not unduly worried. He
instructed his lawyer Nargunam to file an appeal to Delhi. When
Delhi
did not bother about "Cinema Thoothu" the resourceful fellow
utilising past expertise forged and fabricated an order renewing
his
license! With this fake document he managed to run his paying
magazine for some
weeks. When the situation turned hot he gave up "Cinema
Thoothu".
He searched around and soon found a tottering weekly named "Hindu
Nesan". Its objective and theme were to uphold Hindu culture,
tradition,
values and such. It suited Lakshmikantham and he took it over.

"Hindu Nesan" clicked at once and its new editor expanded his
target
list of victims. Besides movie people he wrote salacious
scandalous
stories about business magnates, prosperous lawyers and doctors,
popular musicians, feudal landlords and landed gentry, press
barons
and idle rich, religious heads and even small fry if there was a
chance to bleed them. Every scandal tinted with sex was grist to
his
mill and that spelt money.

'Hush money' poured in and soon Lakshmikantham acquired property,
like houses, land and gold and a printing press. Soon he was
elevated to the status of cult figure in Madras. Most of the
stuff
he wrote was
partly true and his writing acquired a sheen of credibility adding
to his popularity. He was smart enough to know that every
cupboard
had its skeletons and he searched for them!

Victims, like Bhagavathar tried to buy his silence but
Lakshmikantham was too smart to yield to such tactics. Some
again,
like Bhagavathar hired hooligans to beat him up, but he the bigger
hooligan of them all
proved too tough, and he beat them up! One such hired goonda was
a
rowdy, a milkman by profession who had supplied milk to the
Lakshmikantham family. His name was Nagalingam.

Lakshmikantham knew he had enemies of many a kind, big and small.
So for a short while he hired a bodyguard to protect him. That
man
was a well- known weightlifter, boxer who ran a gymnasium for
sometime. He was a staunch Hindu and had played a role in Hindu-
Muslim riots. He also played roles in movies as stuntman. His
name
was Sreenivasulu Naidu, better known as Arya Veera Seenan!

"Hindu Nesan" not only became a best-selling weekly but also a
much-
talked about one. Of course, in most homes the crudely produced
weekly printed on cheap paper, the entire contents of which were
written by the wily editor himself was read on the sly, in privacy
like toilets and bedrooms. But almost every adult talked about
the
stories in the weekly, their authenticity and all that. Though
very
few had seen the muck-peddler, he was almost a household name at
least for a brief period.

And then Lakshmikantham announced that a new sensational story
would
be featured from a particular date in his weekly. A no-holds-
barred
expose one a sensational but unsolved killing known as 'The Boat
Mail
Murder'. 'The Boat Mail' was a fast running train from Madras to
Dhanushkodi, serving as a link for ships from India to Sri Lanka.
Hence, the name. In that train, a rich banker, a moneybag,
Lakshmanan
Chettiar, was brutally murdered as the mail steamed its way to
Madras tearing through the dark night. Who killed the wealthy
man?
To this day, half a century later, it remains a mystery,
unsolved.
The murder
rocked the province and many bigwigs were said to be behind it.
The
killing, a bloody one, allegedly took place in the presence of an
eye-witness, a popular singer-actress, but somehow the truth never
surfaced nor was it permitted, the public felt. Lakshmikantham
wrote
that he would reveal the names of the killers in the very first
issue.

That issue of "Hindu Nesan" never came out! Before it could
Lakshmikantham went out of this wide, wicked world and his exit
was
set in motion that day, November 8, 1944!

Lakshmikantham had a tenant living in one of his houses. He was a
hired help in type-composing in one of the top English dailies of
Madras and his widowed sister-in-law kept house for him.
Lakshmikantham took a dislike to his tenant, maybe because the man
came from a lowly depressed community. Anyway he wanted him to
vacate his house but the press-worker refused. Enraged the house-
owner
published titillating tit-bits in 'Hindu Nesan' about the tenant
and
his buxom sister-in-law who the editor suggested in his racy Tamil
took good care of the man of many more things not merely the house!
Consequently the two men fought in streets, exchanging blows with
the scandal-sheet scribe getting knifed in the process. He also
filed a case of trespass and succeeding in it he tried to take
possession of
his house, throwing and breaking things like mirrors, chairs and
pots in the process.

Lakshmikantham filed a criminal complaint against his tenant for
assault but the cops did not take notice of it. So he briefed his
lawyer to file a private complaint against that damned low-born
fellow. His name was Vadivelu.

That fateful morning Lakshmikantham carried legal papers connected
with this case and Nargunam and he discussed the matter, seated in
the upstairs office-room of his bungalow. Then the lawyer took
leave of
his friend asking him to meet him later in court and he went in to
get ready to go to court. Lakshmikantham walked out towards his
rickshaw. The "puller" was missing for a while and then he got
into
the
man-drawn-vehicle, destination home, sweet home.

Maddox street winds along a narrow, lonely deserted curve and ends
in General Collins Road, a quiet street of garden houses and
spacious bungalows only on one side. The other side was vacant
land, and
playgrounds. In 1944 there was not much of traffic in that part
of
Vepery. Madras City, itself was then only a small city of some
half
a million people as against five million and more of today.

While the rickshaw moved down that curve with no houses around,
suddenly some men, - how many nobody was sure ? appeared and
zeroed
in on the vehicle, toppling it. One of them pulled out a knife and
stabbed Lakshmikantham and as suddenly as they appeared they
disappeared running in different directions.

Lakshmikantham bleeding and groaning in severe pain got up and
with
his hands pressed to the wounded area, he walked back to his
lawyer's house. By now, Nargunam who had heard about the stabbing
from some
passers-by had come out of the house. The two met on the street
when Lakshmikantham told him about the attack. What did he say?
According to the lawyer's evidence in court, the Tamil word his
client used was
"Avan" (which means "he"). Whom did he refer to by "he"?

{Years later in the early 1970s when the writer was researching on
this case, Nargunam told him emphatically that the word
Lakshmikantham used was "Evano!" which means "someone"! "Avan"
and "Evan" are
phonetically similar and that's how the confusion arose he
explained. When asked why he did not tell the court, what he told
the writer, Nargunam merely shrugged it all off!}

Anyway, Nargunam put Lakshmikantham in another rickshaw and asked
him to go to the General Hospital, the biggest at that time, a
mile
away. He also requested a client of his, Mr. Brew, an Anglo-
Indian,
who
happened to pass by, to go along with the injured man. Brew
followed in another rickshaw and now Lakshmikantham bleeding and
bound for the General Hospital instructed his rickshaw-man to take
him to the Vepery police station situated nearby.

Sub-inspector Krishnan Nambiyar came out, on being informed by
Brew
and met the bleeding man seated in the vehicle. Now, Brew left
and
went about his work. What exactly took place at the station was
never
clear for the police version was questioned later in court during
the sensational trial and a study of the trial papers does not
convey the impression that the truth did not come out. Or it was
not
permitted to
as many believed then and continue to do so to this day.

According to Nambiyar, Lakshmikantham complained speaking in
English
about the stabbing attack on him and while he spoke Nambiyar took
it
all down on a piece of white paper. This was something unusual
even
odd, for under the police procedure and law, such complaints
should
be written in a book maintained for that purpose and not on bits
of
paper. But the cop had an explanation. As the man was too injured
to
have left the vehicle he took down what he narrated on a sheet of
paper under the circumstances. Nambiyar had to stand, one foot on
the rickshaw, paper and pad resting on his thigh, and write which
would
have been impossible if he had to bring that bulky book and write
it
all down in triplicate using carbon sheets!

After he wrote it all down, Lakshmikantham read it and signed it
at
the bottom, or did he? If so, when and where? Indeed this
important document became an issue later during the trial. And,
not
without
reason!

In that complaint, the name Vadivelu and 'another man', aged 30,
were mentioned as the assailants. Lakshmikantham, however never
identified, nor named his attackers at any time before he died the
morning after. Indeed when he was treated at the out-patient ward
of
the General Hospital by Dr. A. K. Joseph, he stated that he had no
idea of who his attacker was. In the register maintained by the
hospital the relevant
entry read "Name of assailant ? Not known".

Again, later, when Dr. P. R. Balakrishnan, the house surgeon who
treated Lakshmikantham as an in-patient talked to him the 'yellow'
journalist stated he did not know who the assailant was. If so,
how
did the name Vadivelu appear in that sheet?

In Nagalingam, the rowdyish milkman who was later arrested and
charged as the second assailant was really the person, why did
Lakshmikantham mention about a person aged 30. Nagalingam had
been
his milkman and
known him for years. If so, why did not he name Nagalingam as the
second culprit? Was Nagalingam not there that morning? Vadivelu?
Was he there, or not?

To confuse matters there were corrections and over-writings and
such, even inks of different colours in what Nambiyar wrote!

{C.Ramanna, a Madras City lawyer, with a modest practice whom
Vadivelu had consulted about his house tenancy problem told the
writer years later a startling story which he swore was as true
as 'the sun-rise
every dawn'. When Lakshmikantham was stabbed that ill-fated
morning
alleged to be by Vadivelu, the poor press-worker was seated in a
chair in his office! So, the lawyer argued it was impossible that
my client
Vadivelu stabbed that fellow. Why did he not tell the police
about
this steel-strong alibi? The lawyer had a shocking tale to tell?.

He did speak to the city top cops but they shut their ears, eyes
and
all senses. When he persisted they threatened to rope him in,
arrest and charge him as accused. How? The cops had a theory.
Lakshmikantham wrote about a newspaper baron and his affair with a
leading singer-actress and that VIP and this lawyer were related.
Besides, Vadivelu worked in that big shot's press! So, the cops
hinted the baron had asked his lawyer relative to rub off
Lakshmikantham and he in turn fixed up his client Vadivelu and
he?!
The shocked lawyer raced back home and went about his other
business!}.

And that was not all. There were more matters creating and
casting
long and strong shadows of doubts in the police and prosecution
version of the case.

For example, the weapon, a knife, or dagger as it was described.
According to the rickshawman, Gopal, he picked up the blood-
stained
knife and the legal papers Lakshmikantham had with him, equally
stained and took them to Nargunam. Strangely, the lawyer took the
papers and told Gopal to hand over the knife at the Vepery police
station. Why did he keep back the papers which were also important
evidence, like the knife? Nargunam could not offer any valid
explanation either during the trial in the witness-box or years
later to the writer.

An important point he did clarify to the writer was his conduct
after he saw his client with the bleeding stab injuries.
Lakshmikantham had been his friend and client, and a frequent
visitor to his house. Why,
then did he not take him to the nearest doctor at once, or having
failed to do so, why did he not accompany his friend and client to
the General Hospital instead of sending Brew, ho came along that
way
by
sheer accident? He was cross-examined during the trial by defense
lawyers but his answer was far from convincing. Years later he
gave
the writer a convincing explanation. He found that the injuries
were
simple and certainly not so serious to warrant his personal and
immediate attention. Moreover, he was anxious to avoid getting
involved with sordid attacks on a shady character which he knew his
client certainly was!

His evaluation of the injuries as simple tallied with that of S.
I.
Nambiyar's. And, also that of the victim himself. The police
officer had noted in the case register the nature of the injuries
as
simple,
and he registered a case only for 'simple hurt' under the Indian
Penal Code.

Lakshmikantham too should have felt the same way explaining why he
chose to visit the police station first without rushing to the
Hospital as urged by his lawyer.

Dr. Joseph too had the same opinion when he treated the injured
man
at the out-patient ward.

Another issue of confusion, - and doubt -, was the knife.
According
to the police case, Gopal handed over the weapon to Nambiyar.
Under
the rules the receipt of the knife should have been entered in a
property register, immediately by the station clerk. But no such
entry about this knife was found noted in that book! Why not?
Nambiyar offered vague replies under cross-examination and
ventured
to
suggest that such procedure was not always followed! A perusal of
the Vepery police station register revealed that even
insignificant
items, like onions, eggs and such were recorded, but this blood-
stained knife
was not. Why not? Was there no such knife at all? Were Gopal and
Nambiyar spinning yarns? Was the knife a fake, not the real one,
but something introduced later by the cops?

What of Gopal? Was he really an eye-witness to the attack, as he
claimed to be. The defense case was that he had run away, scared
to
death as soon as his rickshaw was toppled. There is strong force
behind this theory. After Lakshmikantham was stabbed he a man
with
knife injuries bleeding and shocked came walking groaning and
moaning towards his lawyer's house. Why did he not come in that
rickshaw.
Was it because that Gopal was not around to help the victim,
having
run away to safety? Indeed Gopal came with his vehicle towards
the
place on the street where the lawyer and the victim stood only
minutes
later. All these suggest strongly that Gopal was no eye-witness
as
put by the police and his evidence not based on truth.

However, Lakshmikantham accompanied by a cop sent by Nambiyar
reached the General Hospital where Dr. Joseph attended on him and
also spoke about the attack based on which he made those entries
in
the
'casualty' register. Interestingly the prosecution did not choose
to call Dr. Joseph to give evidence at the trial in spite of the
pressure by the presiding judge, Mr. Justice Vere Mockett (later
Sir
Vere
Mockett). Somewhat exasperated the honorable High Court Judge
summoned this young doctor to as a court witness to tender
evidence.
Why did the prosecution fight shy? The explanation was
unconvincing.

At the GH Lakshmikantham was beginning to feel uneasy and the
bleeding from the injury in the abdomen did not cease. So he was
admitted as an in-patient for further treatment in the Wenlock
ward
under a
well-known surgeon of the day, Dr. Nadkarni Mangesh Rao, one of
the
senior Indian doctors of Madras.

Around this period Nargunam visited the hospital twice. He
deposed
in court that the first time he found that his friend had not
reached the hospital and on the second visit, Lakshmikantham was
in
poor condition
and taking a turn for the worse.

{However, some quarter-century later the lawyer told the writer a
different tale which he declared was the undiluted truth. When he
went to the GH, he did meet Lakshmikantham whom he found in bed but
hale and hearty. He cracked obscene jokes in Tamil about
those "sons
of whores" who dared to attack him. He would get out of GH in a
day
or two and would teach all those bastards a fitting lesson and show
who Lakshmikantham really was! Nargunam laughed enjoying his
friend's
two-lettered Tamil abuses and left promising to visit him next
morning
on his way to court. His shock when he heard about
Lakshmikantham's
death knew no bounds for he never expected it. "I never even
dreamt
he would die!" he remarked his voice dropping to a heavy whisper.}

Whatever happened to Lakshmikantham at the GH? Something that
ended
his eventful, exciting life. Something, now very clear, discussed
hotly then, both in and out of courts and even today almost half a
century later.

When the bleeding did not stop Dr. Mangesh Rao wondered if the
patient
had suffered any serious internal injury like damage to the
peritoneum. He felt an exploration of the abdominal wound was
necessary. So he instructed his assistant Dr. P. R. Balakrishnan
to
do the needful as he was busy otherwise engaged in serious cases!
But
the problem Dr. Balakrishnan found that the blood pressure of the
patient had dropped very low and promptly he informed his senior of
the condition. Dr. Rao felt that an exploration of the wound under
such low blood pressure would prove fatal and told his assistant
against it and suggested some treatment to stop the bleeding.

Yet, Dr. Balakrishnan did perform an exploration on Lakshmikantham.
Why did he do it? No answer was ever found and Dr. Balakrishnan
carried the truth to his grave. He did offer an explanation but it
satisfied none. Anyway his surgical exploration created another
wound, the second one. This two-wound situation figured
prominently
at the trial casting doubts on the prosecution case. Indeed the
defense openly suggested that the cause of the death was the
exploration and not the stab wound.

An examination of the patient's urine revealed blood, indicating
damage to the kidney. There was no clear proof about the
condition
of
the urine before Lakshmikantham was wheeled into the operation
theater. What had caused the damage? The surgical exploration?

Interestingly and perhaps, sadly, no defense lawyer categorically
asked questions whether the blade of the knife could have reached
the
kidney and cut it into two! All the counsel were very eminent
lawyers
of standing, skills and experience but nobody pursued that line.
One
wonders why?

The news about the attack on Lakshmikantham hit the city like a
storm
and crowds in large numbers gathered inside the hospital grounds,
corridors and almost everywhere and police had a tough time
maintaining order. It became the talk of the town, and wild
stories
began to circulate about the identity of the assailant or
assailants,
nobody was sure, not even the cops at this juncture ? and the
people
behind it.

As night fell, Lakshmikantham took a turn for the worse. He became
delirious and in the early hours of the following morn, at about
4.15,
November 9, 1944, he died, due to secondary shock caused by damage
to
the kidney. It was all over. A life of stir and sensation,
excitement and escapes had ended. An ex-con who had been a thorn
in
many a flesh, had been eliminated. A case of simple hurt had
ended
in
murder. A hornet's nest had been stirred.

Who did it? Nobody really knows.

Lakshmikantham had been elevated to the status of a martyr. His
death
rocked south India, and his funeral became an event. His body was
laid on a bench in the front area of his house in Purasawalkam for
public view, and thousands of men, women and even kids thronged to
pay
homage to him. Floral wreaths were laid at his feet, and many
women
who had never known or seen him wept beating their breasts wailing
loudly. Mass hysteria reigned during the funeral. A poor man, an
ordinary man, who took cudgels to cleanse society had been
eliminated
by the sinful haves, the sadistic rich, the bigwigs. That was the
feeling that filled the air, as thousands followed the funeral
procession. That day, C. N. Lakshmikantham, dead and cold, was the
most talked-about person in Madras. A raging world war, a voilent
freedom struggle, shortage of essential commodities, all were
forgotten by people.

Police Inspector Keshava Menon took over the investigation of the
murder at once. Frankly he had no clue of any kind. Stories
circulated in the Maddox street ? General Collins Road area of a
group
of three persons, or four was seen running around the time of
attack.
And the hunt for that 'gang of three' began in right earnest!

The police had known about the enemity between the murdered man,
and
the press-worker, Vadivelu. So, Menon arrested him quickly and
began
to work on the case. Before he did so, the cops interrogated many
and
for a while any group of three found walking in Vepery was treated
as
suspects and interrogated. Keystonish? Yet true. The answer was
the
cops had no clue. None at all.

Lakshmikantham had many enemies. Big and small. He had known that
his life was in danger.

[In a chat with the writer during his research, Nargunam narrated
how
on many nights Lakshmikantham had requested his lawyer to walk
along
with him and put him in a rickshaw for he had the uneasy, but
certain
feeling that some tough-looking fellows, total strangers, were

should
do
all he could to help his aged mother and an elder brother who had
been
blind for years. Lakshmikantham was greatly attached to these two
and
looked after them well.

His wife never got on with her aged mother-in-law and took her to
court for property disputes, even during the murdered man's
lifetime.
There were strong rumours that Lakshmikantham was never legally
married.]

C. V. Arunagiri Mudaliar, then Assistant Commissioner of Police,
Madras was put in charge of the case by the top cop of the city, a
Britisher R. C. Hume. Mudaliar, Menon and the investigating team
realised that they had a difficult assignment to handle. Vadivelu
offered them hope and they came to know the police, under the law
is
not bound to its sources of information that Vadivelu and two
others
were seen walking together in Vepery. The 'gang of three'? The
other
two men were traced and kept in the police custody. The two men, a
kind of do-gooders belonging to the same depressed classes as
Vadivelu
did, were trying to help the poor press-worker in his legal
tangles.
They were benumbed with shock and stark fear when they realised
that
they were suspected to be involved in the murder! They were put up
for identification at parades and made bold to send petitions to
the
higher-ups seeking justice. After a nerve-racking time they wre
let
off as being innocent! But Vadivelu was kept back. And, there
for a
while the investigation stopped. The cops could not proceed
further?
No clues, nothing could be found.

Meanwhile public opinion about the delay in the police
investigation
began to gather strength. Certain groups in the city held
meetings
to
condemn the murder of a man who they considered as a moral
crusader,
and motives were attributed for the police tardiness, and the
inability of the famed Madras City police to ban the real culprits.
The police was accused of shielding the big shots of Madras who
were
said to be the actual culprits.

Thus the Madras city police was under severe pressure from many
quarters and the heat scaled the high point when a memorandum was
presented to Sir Arthur Hope, by an association of government
employees. The Governor of Madras, in turn, turned the valve on
his
officers and police top brass. Get the killers? get on? get
someone?come on? get on? on?on?

And the police did! Quickly the police arrested three more men,
the
milkman Nagalingam, boxer Arya Veera Seenan, and a butcher,
Rajabathar. Who were these men? According to the police,
Nagalingam
was the other man, along with Vadivelu who attacked Lakshmikantham.
Frankly all of them were small fry, also-rans. According to the
police these were only errand boys. For whom?

A letter gave the cops the lead, or did it? A third-rate
struggling,
self-styled Tamil playwright and so-called screen-writer A. K.
Ramanna, a resident of Purasawalkam, wrote a letter to his friend
in
whose house he was a paying guest, a film actor doing small roles,
V.
S. Mani, then shooting at Salem. The letter, interesting and
curious
read as follows,?"Yesterday morning at about 10.30, CNL while
returning from the house of advocate Narguman in Maddox street was
dastardly stabbed with a 'bichuva' in three places, and he died at
4
o'clock during the early hours of the day? he has been finished by
a
person? Yesterday morning at 11 o'clock, _____ came to me and
informed me that he had done away with the life of CNL and warning
me
not to publish this news went away?"

This letter bore the date November 9, 1944 and reached Salem, some
two
hundred and odd miles away from Madras, only on the morning of
14th.
When Mani received it a police officer, K. V. Venkatsubramaniam,
was
already seated near the actor, waiting to seize it! How? Thereby
hangs a tale, a curious one indeed. Very.

Ramanna's letter posted in Purasawalkam was seen by the Censor who
passed on a photostat copy to the police. During the war, the
British
Indian government had a Censor authorised to intercept and open
letters under the guise of security. But, why was this letter
intercepted, opened and read? Ramanna was no terrorist, no wanted
man
gone underground, no alien. In fact he was a nobody. Why, then
was
his letter opened? Sheet chance? Telepathy? Some other reason?
Or
was the letter a police plant, as suggested by the defense?

Ramanna was easily traced by the city police and taken for
interrogation which it would appear went on for some days during
which
period he was kept in custody. Not strictly legal, perhaps, but
that's how thing were in British-ruled India.

Who was the person whose name Ramanna had left blank in his letter?
Arya Veera Seenan, he told the police. Seenan, Mani and he were
old
friends and the famed boxer visited Mani often. Seenan also
disclosed, Ramanna said, that the 'operation Lakshmikantham murder'
was financed and backed by big shots of the Tamil cinema world,
precisly, the reigning superstar M. K. Thyagaraja Bhagavathar,
leading
comedian and star N.S. Krishnan and successful producer-director
S.
M.
Sreeramulu Naidu.

The police also had knowledge of the memorandum submitted to the
Governor of Madras by filmland personalities affected by the
writings
of the murdered muckraker in "Cinema Thoothu". The top three
signatories were MKT Bhagavathar, NSK and SMS Naidu!

Soon the cops picked up another young man, Jayanandan from
Royapuram,
a sea-side area of the city. A waster, goo-for-nothing fellow, he
was
the brother of a buxom, bosomy up and coming Tamil film actress
Clara
alias Chandra about whom Lakshmikantham had written in his
weeklies.
He was arrested, but later he was given 'King's pardon' and taken
as
approver. This man would play a key role in this trial and
provided
the high drama, sensations and such.

[There was a successful Tamil script-writer, a box-office draw in
his
day, famous under his pen-name Ilangoven. He was a person of odd
tastes and hobnobbed with low-types, like rowdies, riff-raff and
all.
He was also a good friend of Lakshmikantham's and Arya Veera
Seenan's. His constant companion, bodyguard, chauffer and
brother-in-law Sriramulu (The writer had two wives, both sisters of
this man's!) told the writer during the late 1970's that much of
the
"news" in "Cinema Thoothu" and "Hindu Nesan" was based on the
information mostly given by Ilangovan. Even the "hush money" deals
were put through the good offices of this box-office-big shot film
writer. Ilangovan and even Sriramulu were interrogated by the city
police and the writer had expected to be arrested for the murder.
Scared he made himself scarce for a while and surfaced only when
the
coast was clear! During his heyday Ilangovan's name in the film
credits meant success and most of Bhagavathar's box-office bonanzas
were written by him. Bhagavathar insisted that Ilangovan be fixed
as
writer before he agreed to sign a film. However, Ilangovan spent
his
last days in poverty, pain and obscurity.)

And then, south India shook during December 1944, when Bhagavathar,
Krishnan and Sreeramulu Naidu were arrested. The arrests,
specially
of the singing superstar, Bhagavathar was then at the peak of his
fame, a larger-than-life figure, a legend in his lifetime almost
diefied by his fans and followers, and such a person to be arrested
like a lowly criminal and accused of murder! To the average
Tamilian
even the very thought was incredible?

However, the police put of a case in court for the murder of
Lakshmikantham, as abetment, conspiracy and such against eight
accused. After the committal proceedings, before the magistrate,
the
history-making 'Lakshmikantham Murder Case' came up for hearing in
April 1945, at the original Criminal Sessions of the Madras High
Court
by Mr. Justice Vere Mockett, a British Barrister who had practised
in
Madras before his elevation to the Bench.

Vadivelu? Nagalingam? Arya Veera Seenan? M. K. Thyagaraja
Bhagavathar?
N. S. Krishnan? S. M. Sreeramulu Naidu? Rajabathar? Arumugam? Eight
accused in all faced the trial. Arumugam was a cop who was
alleged
to
be one of the conspirators.

The trial attracted enormous and unprecedent attention not only in
India, but even beyond like Ceylon (Sri Lanka), Burma, Malaya and
other land's where there was a sizeable Tamil-speaking population.
Newspapers, in English, Tamil, Telugu and other languages published
'verbatim' reports of the trial, day to day. Unmanageable crowds
thronged the famed red-bricked Indo-Saracenic style edifice of the
Madras High Court, eager to take a close look at the popular and
glamorous faces in the dock, M. K. Thyagaraja Bhagavathar, an idol
of
the masses and classes and N. S. Krishnan both legendary figures of
south India.

P. V. Rajamannar, one of the legal luminaries of India, a long time
Chief Justice of Madras, then the Advocate General, led the
prosecution assisted by P. Govinda Menon (later, a judge of the
Madras
High Court, and Supreme Court of India). Arunagiri Mudaliar, who
masterminded the investigation helped the Prosecution counsel.

A battery of lawyers, the cream of the Bar in Madras and beyond
appeared for the accused. Brilliant men of provenmerit, forensic
skills, expertise and experience, like V. T. Rangaswamy Ayyangar,
V.
Rajagopalachari, Braddel (a Barrister from Singapore then in Madas
on
account of the war and Japanese occupation of that city) B. T.
Sundararajan S. Govind Swaminathan and K.M. Munshi (A brilliant
Bombay
lawyer leading Gujerati writer, patriot-pilitician, Indian National
Congress leader and after India became free, central minister and
state governor).

Munshi was brought from Bombay for a fabulous, unheard of fee to
defend Sreeramulu Naidu and his appearance made the trial a 'cause
celebre' all over India. He dominated the team of defense counsel,
and in a brilliant move he raised a plea of 'alibi' for Naidu. He
produced evidence based on the records of Hotel Taj, Bombay, to
show
and prove that during the relevant dates of the conspiracy Naidu
had
stayed at this famous and top hotel. The plea was successful and
the
prosecution filed a "Nolle Prosequi" (no prosecution) resulting in
the
discharge of Naidu. The producer-director-studio-owner from
Coimbatore walked out a free man.

Munshi was then engaged to defend N. S. Krishnan. He and other
counsel fought hard for the defense investing all their talents,
skills, legal acumen.

The trial took an unexpected dramatic turn when the approver
Jayanandam entered the witness box to give evidence. Ever since,
the
police picked him up on November 26th he had been a problem to the
city cops. He expressed his desire to confess before a magistrate
but
did not do so when he was taken for that purpose. Interestingly,
the
police kept him in a prison at Saidapet, then outside the city
limits,
rather strange thing to be done. Finally he confessed on December
22nd and spoke about the murder which he claimed was the truth,
whole
truth and nothing but the truth!

His confession? Nagalingam, his old friend came to him and said
that
arrangements had been made to do away with Lakshmikantham and it
would
be funded by Bhagavathar and N. S. Krishnan. As the scandal-weekly
had published obscene tales about his actress-sister and him, he
had
a
grudge against its editor. So he agreed to join his friend to
murder
Lakshmikantham. On November 7, 1944, he met Nagalingam and also
Vadivelu, Arya Veera Seenan, Rajabathar, Arumugam. He was also
introduced to one Kamalanathan, who would get the money for the
murder. All went to a drama theater, then very famous, called Wall
Tax Theater on Wall Tax Road. Here, they were met by a man with
prominent whiskers who took Jayanandam, Kamalanathan, Seenan and
Nagalingam into the 'green room' (make up room) where Bhagavathar
and
N. S. Krishnan were present. The details of the murder were
discussed
and an advance of Rs.500 was paid. The killers also took an oath
of
secrecy and a ritual of eating betel-leaves and salt was done by
the
men.

Next morning Nagalingam woke him up by 4.45 am, and both went to
Purasawalkam to carry out the "job" All the men assembled and
moved
to General Collins Road where Vadivelu and Nagalingam finished the
"murder mission"? so ran Jayanandam's confession.

And before the Judge and jury the approver went back on his
confession
and told the court on oath that his confession was a tissue of
lies,
lies and lies, containing not even a grain of truth! He had been
tortured and pressurised by the police to confess and state all
that
he did before the magistrate. Taken by shock and surprise, the
Advocate-General had Jayanandam treated as a hostile witness and
cross-examined his own 'star witness'.

The other element of interest was the evidence of the city police
surgeon, a professor of the Madras Medical College, a well-known
person in the medical and social circles of Madras, Dr. A.
Sreenivasulu Naidu. He had performed the post-mortem on the body
and
he underwent, a tough, uncomfortable and embarrassing time in the
witness box. He gave evidence about his post-mortem, the nature of
the injuries, Dr. Balakrishnan's surgical exploration, cause of
death
and connected issues. He was grilled mercilessly during the
cross-examination by Munshi, Rangaswamy Ayyangar, Rajagopalachari
and
others, experts of that fine art. The heated questioning focussed
on
the wound caused by surgery and Dr. Naidu agreed tacitly that the
exploration might have caused the death. Aware of the damage of
his
answers he tried to defend his colleague. But he made a poor
impression as a professional witness in spite of his credentials,
track record and all. When he left the box, much relieved, he also
left a strong impression that he had not spoken out, as a result of
trying to protect his young colleague Dr. Balakrishnan.

The prosecution let in a long procession of witnesses like Gopal,
A.
K. Ramanna, V. S. Man? his actress wife Jayagouri? milkmen who
claimed
to be seen Jayanandam and others at the scene of crime? Margunam,
Dr.
Mangesh Rao? Dr. Balakrishnan? Kamalanathan? and many others. A
perusal of the trial records does give the uneasy but distinct
feeling
that most witnesses were speaking about events they did not know
much
about. There was loads of prevarication, by most, and the air and
touch of truth seemed missing. Questions in cross-examination were
rarely answered directly and there was a great deal of beating
about
the bush.

The defense too produced witnesses and some of them went into the
box
to support a plea of 'alibi' raised by N. S. Krishnan who claimed
to
have been in Salem, working on a film called "Burma Rani" (1944) at
the Modern Theatres studio, on November 7th, the day of the Wall
Tax
theatre-make-up room meeting.

After a prolonged and much publicised trial, addresses to the jury
by
counsel and the judge, the jury. And much to the surprise of many
in
court and elsewhere, the jury returned a verdict of 'guilty' in
favour
of Arumugam. The verdict shocked Bhagavathar and NSK. Indeed the
famous comedian had expected to get a favourable verdict and his
star
wife, top comedienne, T. A. Mathuram had even made arrangements for
distribution of sugar and sweets and a reception.

The Judge accepted the verdict and awarded life imprisonment to all
the accused. Appeals were filed and heard by a Bench of the High
Court consisting of Sir Lionel Leach, Chief Justice, and Mr.
Justice
K. P. Lakshmana Rao. By now P. V. Rajamannar had become a judge
and
his successor as Advocate-General, K. Rajan Iyer appeared for the
Crown. Again a galaxy of lawyers appeared for the accused-
appellants,
with V. V. Srinivasa Ayyangar, and V. C. Gopalratnam joining the
team,
Munshi was not in the team. VVS was one of the leading lawyers of
Madras, enjoying a fabulous income. A man of culture, with a flair
for fine arts, and he was a High Court Judge for a brief while, and
resigned because the salary was not enough for him to live! VCG
(his
son-in-law) also a successful trial lawyer was a playwright, stage
person and actor, prize-winning sportsman and a granary of humour
and
wit. (The writer was his apprentice and his junior in his chambers
for a very brief while, during which he tried to learn
unsuccessfully
and the secret of leading a full life with some success!).

However, the appeals were dismissed and sentences confirmed by the
Appelate Bench and the accused disappeared behind the high walls of
jail.

Yet Bhagavathar and Krishnan would not give up. They filed
appeals
to
the Privy Council in London. D. N. Pritt, the internationally
known
British Barrister appeared for the two movie stars. The then
highest
court of appeal felt that the two accused did not have a fair trial
and remanded the case back to India for a fresh look.

Back in Madras, the remanded appeal came up for hearing before a
Bench
of two judges. Mr. Justice Happel, and Mr. Justice Shahbuddin.
V.
L.
Ethiraj, one of the most successful criminal lawyers of India, a
glamorous personality in a unglamorous profession, a Barrister
with a
staggering success ration. (V. C. Gopalratnam wisecracked that V L
stood for 'very lucky') appeared for Bhagavathar and Krishnan.

He built his arguments mainly on the credibility of the approver's
confessional evidence. Jayananam by going back on his confession
and
also his conduct before confession had proved himself to be
thoroughly
unreliable. To base a conviction on the uncorroberated testimony
of
an unreliable witness could be dangerous, Ethiraj argued. He also
stressed that the evidence of Gopal, A. K. Ramanna and others was
unrealiable. Ramann's letter seemed highly improbable for no sane
person would rush to a casual friend and speak bout a murder he had
committed a few hours earlier. Certainly not, a woldlywise man
like
Arya Veera Seenan. If Ramanna were a truthful man why did he leave
the name of the killer blank in that letter. To put in any name
later? Ethiraj also argued that the judge had not properly
directed
the jury and the evidence of doubtful value not properly assessed.

The learned judges agreed with Ethiraj's arguments and acquitted
Bhagavathar and Krishnan. The judgement was delivered in 1947.
Soon,
the other accused were also set free.

Bhagavathar, Krishnan and the other four, Vadivelu, Nagalingam,
Arya
Veera Seenan and Rajabathar came out after a 30 month sojourn in
prison.

The Lakshmikantham Murder Case, the prolonged trial, the prison
term,
appeals' aftermath and all crushed Bhagavathar and virtually tolled
his death knell. He was ruined financially, psychologically and
emotionally and never recovered from the shock. He made many
attempts
to rise life a phoenix, made films but failed miserably. Soon his
health too failed and he passed away in pain and poverty. A tragic
fall indeed for the mightiest Roman of the all, a sad end to a
success
story of incredible kind.

N. S. Krishnan recovered quickly. Indeed he was his usual cheerful
self even during the trial, and in prison, cracking jokes and
performing a brilliant 'mimicry' show imitating lawyers, jury and
judge. Even K. M. Munshi was not spared, which that creative,
artistic Bombay lawyer enjoyed most, laughing uproariously! After
his
release, Krishnan's career zoomed, with his own films, some of
which
he also directed. He dabbled in politics, and his clout, cult
figure
status and such helped to boost his career. But somehow things
were
not the same as the gold old days. Within a decade, he too went
down
hill and dies in 1957. He died almost penniless.

Vadivelu never recovered from the shock and trauma that affected
his
mind. He died some years ago. Nagalingam committed suicide,
hanging
himself from a ceiling fan. Rajabathar eked out a precarious living
selling beef in Purasawalkam market and died some years ago.

(The writer made many attempts to meet him and make him talk but he
made himself scarce. He felt that the writer was trying to send
him
to prison again!).

Only Arya Veera Seenan lived long , old and weary and bitter
about
life and its cruelty. He denied he had anything to do with the
murder.

Jayanandam was charged with perjury and sentenced to a term in
jail.
He too committed suicide some years ago.

Experts and even lay-men of those times feel that the truth about
the
murder did not come out, and Bhagavathar, N. S. Krishnan and others
were poor victims of the British Indian Rulers, who used these
ill-fated men as pawns in a game of power politics. Indeed one of
the
High Court judges who heard the remanded appeal remarked in open
court
that on the evidence even a rat could not be convicted.

The real killers were never caught and the Truth lies buried
somewhere, to this day. Truth will out, they say, and one like
this
writer wonders when! Perhaps, someday? one day?!

November 8, 1944? Madras, a sea-kissed sunny city founded some 350
plus years ago by an employee of the East India Company, Francis
Day, the provincial capital woke up as usual as the resplendent sun
rose
out of the blue waters of the Bay of Bengal. To one of its
citizens,named C. N. Lakshmikantham, a colorful character of
considerable notoriety, a popular journalist of the 'yellow' variety
it would be his last day on this planet. Only he was blissfully
ignorant of it all. Indeed if someone had told him so that day-
break he would have
laughed it off, mixing it with some rich colorful two-letter Tamil
words (the equivalent of the English four-lettered ones!) and most
likely he would have slapped that bold someone! Such was the man,
the
earliest of yellow journalists, the prince of muck-rakers.

After finishing his mundane morning chores this go-getter was on his
way in a man drawn rickshaw to meet his lawyer an Indian Christian
who looked more like a Shakespearean actor with his wavy hair, long
side-burns and all named J. Nargunam. At that time he had only a
modest practice and lived on Maddox Street, Vepery, not very far
from where Lakshmikantham lived in a narrow house-lined street in
nearby
Purasawalkam. The client and lawyer were good friends and met
almost every evening after court hours.

Lakshmikantham was quite a court-bird. And a big of a jailbird too.
Indeed his pre-journalist life reads like a chapter from a best
selling suspense thriller. He had ambitions of becoming a lawyer,
something he loved but he could not afford to go to Law College.
Yet he did not forget it and read law books. He frequented law
courts of Madras city and hobnobbed with small fry lawyers,
briefless, breadless
ones and litigants of the illiterate kind. Clever and quick-witted
he soon became a law tout. Touts though prohibited by law are men
who take cases and other legal matters to lawyers for a substantial
'kick-back' in the fee. Patronised by briefless and struggling
lawyers some touts in Madras with a smattering of legal knowledge
earn more than most lawyers. They draft deeds and documents, wills
and
notices mostly for illiterates and some touts resort to forgery and
fabricating documents very casually of course, for a stiff fee.

Lakshmikantham was one such tout and smart enough to argue his own
cases in court. One of his shady deals involving the fabrication of
a sworn affidavit created by him in a case filed against him by a
leading Madras daily found him in the dock in the Madras High Court
Original Sessions. During the hearing he escaped hoodwinking a
posse of cops and for a while he lived a happy-go-lucky life, until
the cops
nabbed and brought him to trial. Found guilty he received a 7 year
jail sentence and was ordered to be imprisoned at Rajamundry, then
in Madras presidency and now in Andhra Pradesh. While the train
taking
him was crossing a river in Andhra he, hand-chained, managed to
escape fooling the guarding cops and jumped from the chug-chugging
train into the river below shrouded by darkness. With chains and
all he swam
across the big river and disappeared into the dark night. He roamed
around freely for a long while until an angry girl friend tipped off
the searching Madras city cops. Back in custody he was declared
a 'dangerous convict' and deported to the Andamans. With an ocean
surrounding this famed island prison, Lakshmikantham could not even
dream of escape. He served his term until as a war security measure
he along with others was set fee and shipped back home.

Back in Madras, a notorious ex-convict, he avoided courts and cops
for whom he had an understandable allergy! Life had to go on and he
dabbled in many things. He ran a small restaurant serving meat
dishes
which in south India were then known as "military hotels" {Eateries
serving only vegetarian food were called 'civil' hotels}. He lost
in this venture and looked around for greener pastures. Soon he
found
one, Journalism. A profession that would bring him money, fame or
more precisely notoriety, and a violent end.

Around 1943 he launched a film weekly in Tamil "Cinema Thoothu". It
was a different kind of magazine. Lakshmikantham did not publish
articles about theories and trends in cinema and such. He filled the
pages with tales of bedroom activities, amours and amatory
adventures of prominent filmland figures like reigning superstar M.
K. Thyagaraja Bhagavathar, brilliant comedian hailed as the 'Charlie
Chaplin of
India', N. S. Krishnan and his star wife T. A. Mathuram, successful
producer S. M. Sreeramulu Naidu and many others enjoying stardom and
immense popularity in south India. He ran "series" about
Bhagavathar and Krishnan as a weekly feature. He wielded a facile
pen and his writing was lively, punchy and enjoyable to read.
Bhagavathar, a singing star was then at his zenith of his career and
fame. More a singer than an actor his following was incredible.
Masses, classes, intellectuals, upper crust folks, all loved him,
his music. Women
adored him and many society dames threw themselves at him. Such was
his charisma. At this time he was one of the most famous figures of
south India, a household name.

No wonder, "Cinema Thoothu", the first of its kind was a raving sell-
out and copies sold at a premium. The Tamil-speaking world had
never seen anything like this weekly. The south Indian society, some
forty and odd years ago was conservative, old-fashioned and subjects
like sex were strictly taboo and barely whispered. In such rigid,
regimented atmosphere not surprisingly people loved gossip and
scandal
and hungered to read and know all about the goings-on behind closed
doors in high places. Movie glamour had already seeped into the
skin and soul of society and this wily pen-totter took a hundred-and-
ten
per cent advantage of it all.

What was Lakshmikantham's aim in running such a magazine? He
professed, at least in public to be a traditionalist with a
missionary zeal to uphold the ideals and high values of ancient
Indian culture.
He claimed he was socially conscious reformer come to earth, to
clean the stables free of sinners and seducers, adulterers and the
likes. But his real aim, the sole purpose was blackmail, pure and
simple.
And surely it paid him ample dividends. Many actors, actresses and
cinema men paid him hush money to buy his silence.

Some like Bhagavathar complained to the authorities and a memorandum
signed by the superstar and other victims of the blackmailer's
vitriolic pen was submitted to Sir Arthur Hope the then Governor of
Madras. At once he acted and the license for publication was
cancelled. However Lakshmikantham was not unduly worried. He
instructed his lawyer Nargunam to file an appeal to Delhi. When
Delhi
did not bother about "Cinema Thoothu" the resourceful fellow
utilising past expertise forged and fabricated an order renewing his
license! With this fake document he managed to run his paying
magazine for some
weeks. When the situation turned hot he gave up "Cinema Thoothu".
He searched around and soon found a tottering weekly named "Hindu
Nesan". Its objective and theme were to uphold Hindu culture,
tradition,
values and such. It suited Lakshmikantham and he took it over.

"Hindu Nesan" clicked at once and its new editor expanded his target
list of victims. Besides movie people he wrote salacious scandalous
stories about business magnates, prosperous lawyers and doctors,
popular musicians, feudal landlords and landed gentry, press barons
and idle rich, religious heads and even small fry if there was a
chance to bleed them. Every scandal tinted with sex was grist to his
mill and that spelt money.

'Hush money' poured in and soon Lakshmikantham acquired property,
like houses, land and gold and a printing press. Soon he was
elevated to the status of cult figure in Madras. Most of the stuff
he wrote was
partly true and his writing acquired a sheen of credibility adding
to his popularity. He was smart enough to know that every cupboard
had its skeletons and he searched for them!

Victims, like Bhagavathar tried to buy his silence but
Lakshmikantham was too smart to yield to such tactics. Some again,
like Bhagavathar hired hooligans to beat him up, but he the bigger
hooligan of them all
proved too tough, and he beat them up! One such hired goonda was a
rowdy, a milkman by profession who had supplied milk to the
Lakshmikantham family. His name was Nagalingam.

Lakshmikantham knew he had enemies of many a kind, big and small.
So for a short while he hired a bodyguard to protect him. That man
was a well- known weightlifter, boxer who ran a gymnasium for
sometime. He was a staunch Hindu and had played a role in Hindu-
Muslim riots. He also played roles in movies as stuntman. His name
was Sreenivasulu Naidu, better known as Arya Veera Seenan!

"Hindu Nesan" not only became a best-selling weekly but also a much-
talked about one. Of course, in most homes the crudely produced
weekly printed on cheap paper, the entire contents of which were
written by the wily editor himself was read on the sly, in privacy
like toilets and bedrooms. But almost every adult talked about the
stories in the weekly, their authenticity and all that. Though very
few had seen the muck-peddler, he was almost a household name at
least for a brief period.

And then Lakshmikantham announced that a new sensational story would
be featured from a particular date in his weekly. A no-holds-barred
expose one a sensational but unsolved killing known as 'The Boat Mail
Murder'. 'The Boat Mail' was a fast running train from Madras to
Dhanushkodi, serving as a link for ships from India to Sri Lanka.
Hence, the name. In that train, a rich banker, a moneybag,
Lakshmanan
Chettiar, was brutally murdered as the mail steamed its way to
Madras tearing through the dark night. Who killed the wealthy man?
To this day, half a century later, it remains a mystery, unsolved.
The murder
rocked the province and many bigwigs were said to be behind it. The
killing, a bloody one, allegedly took place in the presence of an
eye-witness, a popular singer-actress, but somehow the truth never
surfaced nor was it permitted, the public felt. Lakshmikantham wrote
that he would reveal the names of the killers in the very first
issue.

That issue of "Hindu Nesan" never came out! Before it could
Lakshmikantham went out of this wide, wicked world and his exit was
set in motion that day, November 8, 1944!

Lakshmikantham had a tenant living in one of his houses. He was a
hired help in type-composing in one of the top English dailies of
Madras and his widowed sister-in-law kept house for him.
Lakshmikantham took a dislike to his tenant, maybe because the man
came from a lowly depressed community. Anyway he wanted him to
vacate his house but the press-worker refused. Enraged the house-
owner
published titillating tit-bits in 'Hindu Nesan' about the tenant and
his buxom sister-in-law who the editor suggested in his racy Tamil
took good care of the man of many more things not merely the house!
Consequently the two men fought in streets, exchanging blows with
the scandal-sheet scribe getting knifed in the process. He also
filed a case of trespass and succeeding in it he tried to take
possession of
his house, throwing and breaking things like mirrors, chairs and
pots in the process.

Lakshmikantham filed a criminal complaint against his tenant for
assault but the cops did not take notice of it. So he briefed his
lawyer to file a private complaint against that damned low-born
fellow. His name was Vadivelu.

That fateful morning Lakshmikantham carried legal papers connected
with this case and Nargunam and he discussed the matter, seated in
the upstairs office-room of his bungalow. Then the lawyer took
leave of
his friend asking him to meet him later in court and he went in to
get ready to go to court. Lakshmikantham walked out towards his
rickshaw. The "puller" was missing for a while and then he got into
the
man-drawn-vehicle, destination home, sweet home.

Maddox street winds along a narrow, lonely deserted curve and ends
in General Collins Road, a quiet street of garden houses and
spacious bungalows only on one side. The other side was vacant
land, and
playgrounds. In 1944 there was not much of traffic in that part of
Vepery. Madras City, itself was then only a small city of some half
a million people as against five million and more of today.

While the rickshaw moved down that curve with no houses around,
suddenly some men, - how many nobody was sure ? appeared and zeroed
in on the vehicle, toppling it. One of them pulled out a knife and
stabbed Lakshmikantham and as suddenly as they appeared they
disappeared running in different directions.

Lakshmikantham bleeding and groaning in severe pain got up and with
his hands pressed to the wounded area, he walked back to his
lawyer's house. By now, Nargunam who had heard about the stabbing
from some
passers-by had come out of the house. The two met on the street
when Lakshmikantham told him about the attack. What did he say?
According to the lawyer's evidence in court, the Tamil word his
client used was
"Avan" (which means "he"). Whom did he refer to by "he"?

{Years later in the early 1970s when the writer was researching on
this case, Nargunam told him emphatically that the word

and "Evan" are
phonetically similar and that's how the confusion arose he

the writer, Nargunam merely shrugged it all off!}

Anyway, Nargunam put Lakshmikantham in another rickshaw and asked
him to go to the General Hospital, the biggest at that time, a mile
away. He also requested a client of his, Mr. Brew, an Anglo-Indian,
who
happened to pass by, to go along with the injured man. Brew
followed in another rickshaw and now Lakshmikantham bleeding and
bound for the General Hospital instructed his rickshaw-man to take
him to the Vepery police station situated nearby.

Sub-inspector Krishnan Nambiyar came out, on being informed by Brew
and met the bleeding man seated in the vehicle. Now, Brew left and
went about his work. What exactly took place at the station was
never
clear for the police version was questioned later in court during
the sensational trial and a study of the trial papers does not
convey the impression that the truth did not come out. Or it was not
permitted to
as many believed then and continue to do so to this day.

According to Nambiyar, Lakshmikantham complained speaking in English
about the stabbing attack on him and while he spoke Nambiyar took it
all down on a piece of white paper. This was something unusual even
odd, for under the police procedure and law, such complaints should
be written in a book maintained for that purpose and not on bits of
paper. But the cop had an explanation. As the man was too injured to
have left the vehicle he took down what he narrated on a sheet of
paper under the circumstances. Nambiyar had to stand, one foot on
the rickshaw, paper and pad resting on his thigh, and write which
would
have been impossible if he had to bring that bulky book and write it
all down in triplicate using carbon sheets!

After he wrote it all down, Lakshmikantham read it and signed it at
the bottom, or did he? If so, when and where? Indeed this
important document became an issue later during the trial. And, not
without
reason!

In that complaint, the name Vadivelu and 'another man', aged 30,
were mentioned as the assailants. Lakshmikantham, however never
identified, nor named his attackers at any time before he died the
morning after. Indeed when he was treated at the out-patient ward of
the General Hospital by Dr. A. K. Joseph, he stated that he had no
idea of who his attacker was. In the register maintained by the
hospital the relevant
entry read "Name of assailant ? Not known".

Again, later, when Dr. P. R. Balakrishnan, the house surgeon who
treated Lakshmikantham as an in-patient talked to him the 'yellow'
journalist stated he did not know who the assailant was. If so, how
did the name Vadivelu appear in that sheet?

In Nagalingam, the rowdyish milkman who was later arrested and
charged as the second assailant was really the person, why did
Lakshmikantham mention about a person aged 30. Nagalingam had been
his milkman and
known him for years. If so, why did not he name Nagalingam as the
second culprit? Was Nagalingam not there that morning? Vadivelu?
Was he there, or not?

To confuse matters there were corrections and over-writings and
such, even inks of different colours in what Nambiyar wrote!

{C.Ramanna, a Madras City lawyer, with a modest practice whom
Vadivelu had consulted about his house tenancy problem told the
writer years later a startling story which he swore was as true
as 'the sun-rise
every dawn'. When Lakshmikantham was stabbed that ill-fated morning
alleged to be by Vadivelu, the poor press-worker was seated in a
chair in his office! So, the lawyer argued it was impossible that
my client
Vadivelu stabbed that fellow. Why did he not tell the police about
this steel-strong alibi? The lawyer had a shocking tale to tell?.

He did speak to the city top cops but they shut their ears, eyes and
all senses. When he persisted they threatened to rope him in,
arrest and charge him as accused. How? The cops had a theory.
Lakshmikantham wrote about a newspaper baron and his affair with a
leading singer-actress and that VIP and this lawyer were related.
Besides, Vadivelu worked in that big shot's press! So, the cops
hinted the baron had asked his lawyer relative to rub off
Lakshmikantham and he in turn fixed up his client Vadivelu and he?!
The shocked lawyer raced back home and went about his other
business!}.

And that was not all. There were more matters creating and casting
long and strong shadows of doubts in the police and prosecution
version of the case.

For example, the weapon, a knife, or dagger as it was described.
According to the rickshawman, Gopal, he picked up the blood-stained
knife and the legal papers Lakshmikantham had with him, equally
stained and took them to Nargunam. Strangely, the lawyer took the
papers and told Gopal to hand over the knife at the Vepery police
station. Why did he keep back the papers which were also important
evidence, like the knife? Nargunam could not offer any valid
explanation either during the trial in the witness-box or years
later to the writer.

An important point he did clarify to the writer was his conduct
after he saw his client with the bleeding stab injuries.
Lakshmikantham had been his friend and client, and a frequent
visitor to his house. Why,
then did he not take him to the nearest doctor at once, or having
failed to do so, why did he not accompany his friend and client to
the General Hospital instead of sending Brew, ho came along that way
by
sheer accident? He was cross-examined during the trial by defense
lawyers but his answer was far from convincing. Years later he gave
the writer a convincing explanation. He found that the injuries were
simple and certainly not so serious to warrant his personal and
immediate attention. Moreover, he was anxious to avoid getting
involved with sordid attacks on a shady character which he knew his
client certainly was!

His evaluation of the injuries as simple tallied with that of S. I.
Nambiyar's. And, also that of the victim himself. The police
officer had noted in the case register the nature of the injuries as
simple,
and he registered a case only for 'simple hurt' under the Indian
Penal Code.

Lakshmikantham too should have felt the same way explaining why he
chose to visit the police station first without rushing to the
Hospital as urged by his lawyer.

Dr. Joseph too had the same opinion when he treated the injured man
at the out-patient ward.

Another issue of confusion, - and doubt -, was the knife. According
to the police case, Gopal handed over the weapon to Nambiyar. Under
the rules the receipt of the knife should have been entered in a
property register, immediately by the station clerk. But no such
entry about this knife was found noted in that book! Why not?
Nambiyar offered vague replies under cross-examination and ventured
to
suggest that such procedure was not always followed! A perusal of
the Vepery police station register revealed that even insignificant
items, like onions, eggs and such were recorded, but this blood-
stained knife
was not. Why not? Was there no such knife at all? Were Gopal and
Nambiyar spinning yarns? Was the knife a fake, not the real one,
but something introduced later by the cops?

What of Gopal? Was he really an eye-witness to the attack, as he
claimed to be. The defense case was that he had run away, scared to
death as soon as his rickshaw was toppled. There is strong force
behind this theory. After Lakshmikantham was stabbed he a man with
knife injuries bleeding and shocked came walking groaning and
moaning towards his lawyer's house. Why did he not come in that
rickshaw.
Was it because that Gopal was not around to help the victim, having
run away to safety? Indeed Gopal came with his vehicle towards the
place on the street where the lawyer and the victim stood only
minutes
later. All these suggest strongly that Gopal was no eye-witness as
put by the police and his evidence not based on truth.

However, Lakshmikantham accompanied by a cop sent by Nambiyar
reached the General Hospital where Dr. Joseph attended on him and
also spoke about the attack based on which he made those entries in
the
'casualty' register. Interestingly the prosecution did not choose
to call Dr. Joseph to give evidence at the trial in spite of the
pressure by the presiding judge, Mr. Justice Vere Mockett (later Sir
Vere
Mockett). Somewhat exasperated the honorable High Court Judge
summoned this young doctor to as a court witness to tender evidence.
Why did the prosecution fight shy? The explanation was unconvincing.

At the GH Lakshmikantham was beginning to feel uneasy and the
bleeding from the injury in the abdomen did not cease. So he was
admitted as an in-patient for further treatment in the Wenlock ward
under a
well-known surgeon of the day, Dr. Nadkarni Mangesh Rao, one of the
senior Indian doctors of Madras.

Around this period Nargunam visited the hospital twice. He deposed
in court that the first time he found that his friend had not
reached the hospital and on the second visit, Lakshmikantham was in
poor condition
and taking a turn for the worse.

{However, some quarter-century later the lawyer told the writer a
different tale which he declared was the undiluted truth. When he
went to the GH, he did meet Lakshmikantham whom he found in bed but
hale and hearty. He cracked obscene jokes in Tamil about those "sons
of whores" who dared to attack him. He would get out of GH in a day
or two and would teach all those bastards a fitting lesson and show
who Lakshmikantham really was! Nargunam laughed enjoying his
friend's
two-lettered Tamil abuses and left promising to visit him next
morning
on his way to court. His shock when he heard about Lakshmikantham's
death knew no bounds for he never expected it. "I never even dreamt
he would die!" he remarked his voice dropping to a heavy whisper.}

Whatever happened to Lakshmikantham at the GH? Something that ended
his eventful, exciting life. Something, now very clear, discussed
hotly then, both in and out of courts and even today almost half a
century later.

When the bleeding did not stop Dr. Mangesh Rao wondered if the
patient
had suffered any serious internal injury like damage to the
peritoneum. He felt an exploration of the abdominal wound was
necessary. So he instructed his assistant Dr. P. R. Balakrishnan to
do the needful as he was busy otherwise engaged in serious cases!
But
the problem Dr. Balakrishnan found that the blood pressure of the
patient had dropped very low and promptly he informed his senior of
the condition. Dr. Rao felt that an exploration of the wound under
such low blood pressure would prove fatal and told his assistant
against it and suggested some treatment to stop the bleeding.

Yet, Dr. Balakrishnan did perform an exploration on Lakshmikantham.
Why did he do it? No answer was ever found and Dr. Balakrishnan
carried the truth to his grave. He did offer an explanation but it
satisfied none. Anyway his surgical exploration created another
wound, the second one. This two-wound situation figured prominently
at the trial casting doubts on the prosecution case. Indeed the
defense openly suggested that the cause of the death was the
exploration and not the stab wound.

An examination of the patient's urine revealed blood, indicating
damage to the kidney. There was no clear proof about the condition
of
the urine before Lakshmikantham was wheeled into the operation
theater. What had caused the damage? The surgical exploration?

Interestingly and perhaps, sadly, no defense lawyer categorically
asked questions whether the blade of the knife could have reached the
kidney and cut it into two! All the counsel were very eminent
lawyers
of standing, skills and experience but nobody pursued that line. One
wonders why?

The news about the attack on Lakshmikantham hit the city like a storm
and crowds in large numbers gathered inside the hospital grounds,
corridors and almost everywhere and police had a tough time
maintaining order. It became the talk of the town, and wild stories
began to circulate about the identity of the assailant or assailants,
nobody was sure, not even the cops at this juncture ? and the people
behind it.

As night fell, Lakshmikantham took a turn for the worse. He became
delirious and in the early hours of the following morn, at about
4.15,
November 9, 1944, he died, due to secondary shock caused by damage to
the kidney. It was all over. A life of stir and sensation,
excitement and escapes had ended. An ex-con who had been a thorn in
many a flesh, had been eliminated. A case of simple hurt had ended
in
murder. A hornet's nest had been stirred.

Who did it? Nobody really knows.

Lakshmikantham had been elevated to the status of a martyr. His
death
rocked south India, and his funeral became an event. His body was
laid on a bench in the front area of his house in Purasawalkam for
public view, and thousands of men, women and even kids thronged to
pay
homage to him. Floral wreaths were laid at his feet, and many women
who had never known or seen him wept beating their breasts wailing
loudly. Mass hysteria reigned during the funeral. A poor man, an
ordinary man, who took cudgels to cleanse society had been eliminated
by the sinful haves, the sadistic rich, the bigwigs. That was the
feeling that filled the air, as thousands followed the funeral
procession. That day, C. N. Lakshmikantham, dead and cold, was the
most talked-about person in Madras. A raging world war, a voilent
freedom struggle, shortage of essential commodities, all were
forgotten by people.

Police Inspector Keshava Menon took over the investigation of the
murder at once. Frankly he had no clue of any kind. Stories
circulated in the Maddox street ? General Collins Road area of a
group
of three persons, or four was seen running around the time of attack.
And the hunt for that 'gang of three' began in right earnest!

The police had known about the enemity between the murdered man, and
the press-worker, Vadivelu. So, Menon arrested him quickly and began
to work on the case. Before he did so, the cops interrogated many
and
for a while any group of three found walking in Vepery was treated as
suspects and interrogated. Keystonish? Yet true. The answer was
the
cops had no clue. None at all.

Lakshmikantham had many enemies. Big and small. He had known that
his life was in danger.

C. V. Arunagiri Mudaliar, then Assistant Commissioner of Police,
Madras was put in charge of the case by the top cop of the city, a

realised that they had a difficult assignment to handle. Vadivelu

other

Meanwhile public opinion about the delay in the police investigation
began to gather strength. Certain groups in the city held meetings
to
condemn the murder of a man who they considered as a moral crusader,

inability of the famed Madras City police to ban the real culprits.
The police was accused of shielding the big shots of Madras who were
said to be the actual culprits.

Thus the Madras city police was under severe pressure from many

And the police did! Quickly the police arrested three more men, the
milkman Nagalingam, boxer Arya Veera Seenan, and a butcher,

Frankly all of them were small fry, also-rans. According to the
police these were only errand boys. For whom?

person? Yesterday morning at 11 o'clock, _____ came to me and
informed me that he had done away with the life of CNL and warning me
not to publish this news went away?"

This letter bore the date November 9, 1944 and reached Salem, some
two
hundred and odd miles away from Madras, only on the morning of 14th.
When Mani received it a police officer, K. V. Venkatsubramaniam, was
already seated near the actor, waiting to seize it! How? Thereby
hangs a tale, a curious one indeed. Very.

Ramanna's letter posted in Purasawalkam was seen by the Censor who
passed on a photostat copy to the police. During the war, the
British
Indian government had a Censor authorised to intercept and open
letters under the guise of security. But, why was this letter
intercepted, opened and read? Ramanna was no terrorist, no wanted
man
gone underground, no alien. In fact he was a nobody. Why, then was
his letter opened? Sheet chance? Telepathy? Some other reason? Or
was the letter a police plant, as suggested by the defense?

Ramanna was easily traced by the city police and taken for
interrogation which it would appear went on for some days during
which
period he was kept in custody. Not strictly legal, perhaps, but
that's how thing were in British-ruled India.

Who was the person whose name Ramanna had left blank in his letter?
Arya Veera Seenan, he told the police. Seenan, Mani and he were old
friends and the famed boxer visited Mani often. Seenan also
disclosed, Ramanna said, that the 'operation Lakshmikantham murder'
was financed and backed by big shots of the Tamil cinema world,
precisly, the reigning superstar M. K. Thyagaraja Bhagavathar,
leading
comedian and star N.S. Krishnan and successful producer-director S.
M.
Sreeramulu Naidu.

The police also had knowledge of the memorandum submitted to the
Governor of Madras by filmland personalities affected by the writings
of the murdered muckraker in "Cinema Thoothu". The top three
signatories were MKT Bhagavathar, NSK and SMS Naidu!

Soon the cops picked up another young man, Jayanandan from Royapuram,
a sea-side area of the city. A waster, goo-for-nothing fellow, he
was
the brother of a buxom, bosomy up and coming Tamil film actress Clara
alias Chandra about whom Lakshmikantham had written in his weeklies.
He was arrested, but later he was given 'King's pardon' and taken as
approver. This man would play a key role in this trial and provided
the high drama, sensations and such.

[There was a successful Tamil script-writer, a box-office draw in his
day, famous under his pen-name Ilangoven. He was a person of odd
tastes and hobnobbed with low-types, like rowdies, riff-raff and all.
He was also a good friend of Lakshmikantham's and Arya Veera
Seenan's. His constant companion, bodyguard, chauffer and
brother-in-law Sriramulu (The writer had two wives, both sisters of
this man's!) told the writer during the late 1970's that much of the
"news" in "Cinema Thoothu" and "Hindu Nesan" was based on the
information mostly given by Ilangovan. Even the "hush money" deals
were put through the good offices of this box-office-big shot film
writer. Ilangovan and even Sriramulu were interrogated by the city
police and the writer had expected to be arrested for the murder.
Scared he made himself scarce for a while and surfaced only when the
coast was clear! During his heyday Ilangovan's name in the film
credits meant success and most of Bhagavathar's box-office bonanzas
were written by him. Bhagavathar insisted that Ilangovan be fixed as
writer before he agreed to sign a film. However, Ilangovan spent his
last days in poverty, pain and obscurity.)

And then, south India shook during December 1944, when Bhagavathar,
Krishnan and Sreeramulu Naidu were arrested. The arrests, specially
of the singing superstar, Bhagavathar was then at the peak of his
fame, a larger-than-life figure, a legend in his lifetime almost
diefied by his fans and followers, and such a person to be arrested
like a lowly criminal and accused of murder! To the average Tamilian
even the very thought was incredible?

However, the police put of a case in court for the murder of
Lakshmikantham, as abetment, conspiracy and such against eight
accused. After the committal proceedings, before the magistrate, the
history-making 'Lakshmikantham Murder Case' came up for hearing in
April 1945, at the original Criminal Sessions of the Madras High
Court
by Mr. Justice Vere Mockett, a British Barrister who had practised in
Madras before his elevation to the Bench.

Vadivelu? Nagalingam? Arya Veera Seenan? M. K. Thyagaraja
Bhagavathar?
N. S. Krishnan? S. M. Sreeramulu Naidu? Rajabathar? Arumugam? Eight
accused in all faced the trial. Arumugam was a cop who was alleged
to
be one of the conspirators.

The trial attracted enormous and unprecedent attention not only in
India, but even beyond like Ceylon (Sri Lanka), Burma, Malaya and
other land's where there was a sizeable Tamil-speaking population.
Newspapers, in English, Tamil, Telugu and other languages published
'verbatim' reports of the trial, day to day. Unmanageable crowds
thronged the famed red-bricked Indo-Saracenic style edifice of the
Madras High Court, eager to take a close look at the popular and
glamorous faces in the dock, M. K. Thyagaraja Bhagavathar, an idol of
the masses and classes and N. S. Krishnan both legendary figures of
south India.

P. V. Rajamannar, one of the legal luminaries of India, a long time
Chief Justice of Madras, then the Advocate General, led the
prosecution assisted by P. Govinda Menon (later, a judge of the
Madras
High Court, and Supreme Court of India). Arunagiri Mudaliar, who
masterminded the investigation helped the Prosecution counsel.

A battery of lawyers, the cream of the Bar in Madras and beyond
appeared for the accused. Brilliant men of provenmerit, forensic
skills, expertise and experience, like V. T. Rangaswamy Ayyangar, V.
Rajagopalachari, Braddel (a Barrister from Singapore then in Madas on
account of the war and Japanese occupation of that city) B. T.
Sundararajan S. Govind Swaminathan and K.M. Munshi (A brilliant
Bombay
lawyer leading Gujerati writer, patriot-pilitician, Indian National
Congress leader and after India became free, central minister and
state governor).

Munshi was brought from Bombay for a fabulous, unheard of fee to
defend Sreeramulu Naidu and his appearance made the trial a 'cause
celebre' all over India. He dominated the team of defense counsel,
and in a brilliant move he raised a plea of 'alibi' for Naidu. He
produced evidence based on the records of Hotel Taj, Bombay, to show
and prove that during the relevant dates of the conspiracy Naidu had
stayed at this famous and top hotel. The plea was successful and the
prosecution filed a "Nolle Prosequi" (no prosecution) resulting in
the
discharge of Naidu. The producer-director-studio-owner from
Coimbatore walked out a free man.

Munshi was then engaged to defend N. S. Krishnan. He and other
counsel fought hard for the defense investing all their talents,
skills, legal acumen.

The trial took an unexpected dramatic turn when the approver
Jayanandam entered the witness box to give evidence. Ever since, the
police picked him up on November 26th he had been a problem to the
city cops. He expressed his desire to confess before a magistrate
but
did not do so when he was taken for that purpose. Interestingly, the
police kept him in a prison at Saidapet, then outside the city
limits,
rather strange thing to be done. Finally he confessed on December
22nd and spoke about the murder which he claimed was the truth, whole
truth and nothing but the truth!

His confession? Nagalingam, his old friend came to him and said that
arrangements had been made to do away with Lakshmikantham and it
would
be funded by Bhagavathar and N. S. Krishnan. As the scandal-weekly
had published obscene tales about his actress-sister and him, he had
a
grudge against its editor. So he agreed to join his friend to murder
Lakshmikantham. On November 7, 1944, he met Nagalingam and also
Vadivelu, Arya Veera Seenan, Rajabathar, Arumugam. He was also
introduced to one Kamalanathan, who would get the money for the
murder. All went to a drama theater, then very famous, called Wall
Tax Theater on Wall Tax Road. Here, they were met by a man with
prominent whiskers who took Jayanandam, Kamalanathan, Seenan and
Nagalingam into the 'green room' (make up room) where Bhagavathar and
N. S. Krishnan were present. The details of the murder were
discussed
and an advance of Rs.500 was paid. The killers also took an oath of
secrecy and a ritual of eating betel-leaves and salt was done by the
men.

Next morning Nagalingam woke him up by 4.45 am, and both went to
Purasawalkam to carry out the "job" All the men assembled and moved
to General Collins Road where Vadivelu and Nagalingam finished the
"murder mission"? so ran Jayanandam's confession.

And before the Judge and jury the approver went back on his
confession
and told the court on oath that his confession was a tissue of lies,
lies and lies, containing not even a grain of truth! He had been
tortured and pressurised by the police to confess and state all that
he did before the magistrate. Taken by shock and surprise, the
Advocate-General had Jayanandam treated as a hostile witness and
cross-examined his own 'star witness'.

The other element of interest was the evidence of the city police
surgeon, a professor of the Madras Medical College, a well-known
person in the medical and social circles of Madras, Dr. A.
Sreenivasulu Naidu. He had performed the post-mortem on the body and
he underwent, a tough, uncomfortable and embarrassing time in the
witness box. He gave evidence about his post-mortem, the nature of
the injuries, Dr. Balakrishnan's surgical exploration, cause of death
and connected issues. He was grilled mercilessly during the
cross-examination by Munshi, Rangaswamy Ayyangar, Rajagopalachari and
others, experts of that fine art. The heated questioning focussed on
the wound caused by surgery and Dr. Naidu agreed tacitly that the
exploration might have caused the death. Aware of the damage of his
answers he tried to defend his colleague. But he made a poor
impression as a professional witness in spite of his credentials,
track record and all. When he left the box, much relieved, he also
left a strong impression that he had not spoken out, as a result of
trying to protect his young colleague Dr. Balakrishnan.

The prosecution let in a long procession of witnesses like Gopal, A.
K. Ramanna, V. S. Man? his actress wife Jayagouri? milkmen who
claimed
to be seen Jayanandam and others at the scene of crime? Margunam, Dr.
Mangesh Rao? Dr. Balakrishnan? Kamalanathan? and many others. A
perusal of the trial records does give the uneasy but distinct
feeling
that most witnesses were speaking about events they did not know much
about. There was loads of prevarication, by most, and the touch of truth seemed missing. Questions in cross-examination were
rarely answered directly and there was a great deal of beating about
the bush.

The defense too produced witnesses and some of them went into the box
to support a plea of 'alibi' raised by N. S. Krishnan who claimed to
have been in Salem, working on a film called "Burma Rani" (1944) at
the Modern Theatres studio, on November 7th, the day of the Wall Tax
theatre-make-up room meeting.

After a prolonged and much publicised trial, addresses to the jury by
counsel and the judge, the jury. And much to the surprise of many in
court and elsewhere, the jury returned a verdict of 'guilty' in
favour
of Arumugam. The verdict shocked Bhagavathar and NSK. Indeed the
famous comedian had expected to get a favourable verdict and his star
wife, top comedienne, T. A. Mathuram had even made arrangements for
distribution of sugar and sweets and a reception.

The Judge accepted the verdict and awarded life imprisonment to all
the accused. Appeals were filed and heard by a Bench of the High
Court consisting of Sir Lionel Leach, Chief Justice, and Mr. Justice
K. P. Lakshmana Rao. By now P. V. Rajamannar had become a judge and
his successor as Advocate-General, K. Rajan Iyer appeared for the
Crown. Again a galaxy of lawyers appeared for the accused-
appellants,
with V. V. Srinivasa Ayyangar, and V. C. Gopalratnam joining the
team,
Munshi was not in the team. VVS was one of the leading lawyers of
Madras, enjoying a fabulous income. A man of culture, with a flair
for fine arts, and he was a High Court Judge for a brief while, and
resigned because the salary was not enough for him to live! VCG (his
son-in-law) also a successful trial lawyer was a playwright, stage
person and actor, prize-winning sportsman and a granary of humour and
wit. (The writer was his apprentice and his junior in his chambers
for a very brief while, during which he tried to learn unsuccessfully
and the secret of leading a full life with some success!).

However, the appeals were dismissed and sentences confirmed by the
Appelate Bench and the accused disappeared behind the high walls of
jail.

Yet Bhagavathar and Krishnan would not give up. They filed appeals
to
the Privy Council in London. D. N. Pritt, the internationally known
British Barrister appeared for the two movie stars. The then highest
court of appeal felt that the two accused did not have a fair trial
and remanded the case back to India for a fresh look.

Back in Madras, the remanded appeal came up for hearing before a
Bench
of two judges. Mr. Justice Happel, and Mr. Justice Shahbuddin. V.
L.
Ethiraj, one of the most successful criminal lawyers of India, a
glamorous personality in a unglamorous profession, a Barrister with a
staggering success ration. (V. C. Gopalratnam wisecracked that V L
stood for 'very lucky') appeared for Bhagavathar and Krishnan.

He built his arguments mainly on the credibility of the approver's
confessional evidence. Jayananam by going back on his confession and
also his conduct before confession had proved himself to be
thoroughly
unreliable. To base a conviction on the uncorroberated testimony of
an unreliable witness could be dangerous, Ethiraj argued. He also
stressed that the evidence of Gopal, A. K. Ramanna and others was
unrealiable. Ramann's letter seemed highly improbable for no sane
person would rush to a casual friend and speak bout a murder he had
committed a few hours earlier. Certainly not, a woldlywise man like
Arya Veera Seenan. If Ramanna were a truthful man why did he leave
the name of the killer blank in that letter. To put in any name
later? Ethiraj also argued that the judge had not properly directed
the jury and the evidence of doubtful value not properly assessed.

The learned judges agreed with Ethiraj's arguments and acquitted
Bhagavathar and Krishnan. The judgement was delivered in 1947.
Soon,
the other accused were also set free.

Bhagavathar, Krishnan and the other four, Vadivelu, Nagalingam, Arya
Veera Seenan and Rajabathar came out after a 30 month sojourn in
prison.

The Lakshmikantham Murder Case, the prolonged trial, the prison term,
appeals' aftermath and all crushed Bhagavathar and virtually tolled
his death knell. He was ruined financially, psychologically and
emotionally and never recovered from the shock. He made many
attempts
to rise life a phoenix, made films but failed miserably. Soon his
health too failed and he passed away in pain and poverty. A tragic
fall indeed for the mightiest Roman of the all, a sad end to a
success
story of incredible kind.

N. S. Krishnan recovered quickly. Indeed he was his usual cheerful
self even during the trial, and in prison, cracking jokes and
performing a brilliant 'mimicry' show imitating lawyers, jury and
judge. Even K. M. Munshi was not spared, which that creative,
artistic Bombay lawyer enjoyed most, laughing uproariously! After
his
release, Krishnan's career zoomed, with his own films, some of which
he also directed. He dabbled in politics, and his clout, cult figure
status and such helped to boost his career. But somehow things were
not the same as the gold old days. Within a decade, he too went down
hill and dies in 1957. He died almost penniless.

Vadivelu never recovered from the shock and trauma that affected his
mind. He died some years ago. Nagalingam committed suicide, hanging
himself from a ceiling fan. Rajabathar eked out a precarious living
selling beef in Purasawalkam market and died some years ago.

(The writer made many attempts to meet him and make him talk but he
made himself scarce. He felt that the writer was trying to send him
to prison again!).

Only Arya Veera Seenan lived long , old and weary and bitter about
life and its cruelty. He denied he had anything to do with the
murder.

Jayanandam was charged with perjury and sentenced to a term in jail.
He too committed suicide some years ago.

Experts and even lay-men of those times feel that the truth about the
murder did not come out, and Bhagavathar, N. S. Krishnan and others
were poor victims of the British Indian Rulers, who used these
ill-fated men as pawns in a game of power politics. Indeed one of
the
High Court judges who heard the remanded appeal remarked in open
court
that on the evidence even a rat could not be convicted.

The real killers were never caught and the Truth lies buried
somewhere, to this day. Truth will out, they say, and one like this
writer wonders when! Perhaps, someday? one day?!
