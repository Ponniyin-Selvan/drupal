Dear all...

Day 2 (Saturday, February 8th):

Day 2 started in a more leisurely way than Day 1- we started at 7.30,
finished breakfast, and set off on our way to the Periya Koil, the first on
our agenda.

The Periya Koil, as many people have talked about in many different ways, is
a masterpiece. Built by the hero of our story, its size, structure, its
dazzling dimensions have earned it a place in World Heritage. What more can
one say?

In side the temple complex, we split up. Half of us went wandering around
the enormous corridors that surround the temple, some of us ambled over to
the Nandi. Mr.Vishwaksenan disappeared to hunt out the Kalki agent for that
area, and they appeared in a few minutes, right in the middle of a
discussion about the temple.

Mr. Balakumaran had said in his tape that the so-called 'single stone' at
the head of the vimanam wasn't a single stone at all, but was made of many
stones set very close together ("...aaranju chulai maathiri...")- and with
luck, the temple authorities agreed to open up the first level of the
temple- a part which is generally closed to the public, and which requires
special admission if one has to see it. All of us trooped up the stone steps
up to the first level. They led upto a terrace, with the gopuram rising to
our left. The Kalki agent led us to the doorway and unlocked it.

We stepped inside a dark corridor, which ran to our left and right, and
vanished into darkness. Another set of steps led to a doorway which looked
onto a chamber over the garba griham. It was barred at one end (because we
mustn’t stand right on top of the garba griham)- and then the spotlights
were switched on. We raised our heads and looked straight up to the top of
the gopuram. The hollow structure spiralled into the darkness right up to
the very top. It was a magnificent architectural feat. How they ever managed
to do this so many thousands of years ago is simply amazing...

Outside, the dark corridor was lit up, and we could see sculptures detailing
the various dance poses of Lord Nataraja. The panel above the sculptures was
filled with vague colours and outlines. These, we learnt, were paintings
showing King Raja raja and his consorts, and other scenes. Almost nothing
remained of them. The dance poses, on the other hand, were minute- each one
of them was exquisitely detailed. (Dancer Padma Subramanian once visited
this place, it seems, and did some serious research for a whole fortnight).

Some of the dance poses weren't completed- blocks of stone, chiselled and
ready for sculpting, had been left empty- they seemed to waiting for the
hands of a great sculptor to touch them, who would shake them out of their
sleep and convert them into gleaming figurines...

This chamber containing the dance panels, with its dark corridor running
'inside' the gopuram is quite an architectural feat in itself. Its walls,
rising upwards, broaden gradually to meet the outer gopuram at the top.
Magnificent.

After another bout of photographs, we descended to the ground (literally and
imaginatively:-), and began a walking tour of the corridors that surround
the temple. The paintings on the walls had been disfigured (by 'English'
kalvettus, as someone quipped)- but for all that, their beauty was not
impaired. One could only marvel at the vision of Raja raja- not just his
ambition to produce one of the greatest temples in South India, but his
magnanimity in acknowledging the fact that other people (his wives, and
those who had contributed wealth and effort in building the temple) had put
in valuable effort in completing the temple.

"...Shri Raajaraajeeswara mudaiyaarkku naan kuduthanavum akkan kudduthanavum
nam pendugal kuduthanavum matrum kuduthaar kuduthanavum..."

After all, he could just have said that he built the temple, and left it at
that. Who would have dared to oppose him?

(Seems he was taking a bath in the Chozha Maligai in Thanjai, when he
ordered etching inscriptions into stone:-)

After visiting the other sannithis and lounging about the lawns for a while,
we left the temple reluctantly. It was 11 AM when we started towards
Saraswathi Mahal (Periya Koil had made us stay an hour more than we
intended:-). Since we wanted to make good time to Kodikarai, we stayed in
Saraswathi Mahal just long enough to take a sweeping look at it- not more
than half an hour. (what we looked through included the Maratha dresses,
weapons, kitchen utensils and other odds and ends. I especially liked the
'thengai thuruvi':-)

One of the guardian angels of Thanjavur, the 'Nisumba Sudhani' (described by
Balakumaran in his tape as quite a hideous entity) was on our agenda- the
temple still exists, but is known as the 'Vada Badhra kaali (info- Mr.
Sunder Bharadwaj) temple. Since it was nearing twelve, we decided to skip
it. Lunch was finished quickly in Thanjai, and we set off at 1.30 PM - to
Point Calimere.

We reached Kodaikarai by 5 PM ( around 100 Kms). The roads were not too bad,
we made good time, really (Passing the 'Raamar Paadham' on the way, and
uppalams, signaling the approach of the sea). Our first stop, naturally, was
the Kuzhagar Koil. A big, graceful structure with graying walls loomed up in
front of us with a high mathil suvar. Kodikarai proper was 4 kms from the
Koil. (Apparently, Poonghuzhali jumped down from the mathil and sprinted all
the 4kms to the beach. Quite in keeping with her character!)

We took a tour of the temple, which stood majestically, with a few huts at
the side. Thankfully, this was not quite so dilapidated as some
others(probably due to the thiruppani that seems to have been done- in
1940s). There were some obvious additions- the brick-paved floor, for
example. The sculptures surrounding the sannithi were in good condition too.
And this is where we took the photo of the combination lock (which
Krupashankar has posted in his website
(http://www.geocities.com/shankarkrupa/psmy/poottu.jpg)- the one where the
archagar is holding it like an in this week's Kalki issue too- dated 16.02.2003 under the section 'Remote'.
Apparently, it was mentioned in the 'Kalach chuvadu' section in Jaya TV.)

Kamal decided to show off his driving abilities by rushing the van at us:-)-
and we took some photos here too. The approach road to the temple is
well-paved and the whole area is neat and tidy (in spite of scrubby bushes
abounding all over the place)-obviously, it has been re-furbished since
Kalki's times. Kuzhagar is no longer all that alone.

At 5.30 PM, we started towards the beach- and it proved to be the best
evening in the trip:-)

We stopped at the forest department's place first- we needed special
permission to get to the beach. After suitably arranging matters, we took
off. (The beach brought on talk about the trip to Sri Lanka). The road ran
through the village and entered scrubland. After a while we came to a gate,
after passing which, we entered the real Kodikarai- described by Kalki.
Thorny shrubs grew in clumps all over the vast, flatland, stretching away
into the horizon. Deer, grazing patiently, threw startled glances at our
approach, and gambolled into the shrubbery (just as Poonghuzhali saw them).
Some of them suddenly jumped high in the air- we wondered if there were any
'puthai kuzhis' there. The gound seemed firm in most places, and slightly
muddled in others.

The mud road meandered through more shrubs, some growing so thickly that
there were a few possible sites for the 'maraindha mandapam' :-), twisting
and turning. A few cows strayed about lazily. Finally, a few minutes later,
the sea appeared- little waves flowing gently to the shore, and the blue
waters fading into the horizon- exactly as in 'Ponniyin Selvan'.

One could easily picture Vandhiyathevan trying to wash himself in the sea,
desperate to get himself clean:-). There was a lonely brick structure right
on the beach- the remains of some long gone lighthouse of some bygone era,
perhaps. There was a curious hole in it- probably a window embrasure, and we
all took turns, sticking our heads through it.:-)

The setting sun threw its slanting rays through the 'window', and the sea
shore was converted into an idyllic spot, stretching away on all sides. It
was completely empty-except for us. Our first instinct was to get into the
sea, which we did. Krupashankar and Kamal emulated a Veera Saivan and
Vaishnavan-both had their hands on the other's neck- and the former promptly
sat down in the water (which was splendidly cool after the hot drive).

We spent an hour there. splashing through the water to our heart's content.
Almost everyone, I think, pictured Poonghuzhali and her Kolli-vai pisaasus
there:-)- and indulged in a frantic photo-session. God knew what
Krupashankar was trying to do in the sea- we suddenly heard agonized yells
and looked back to see him scrabbling wildly after his slippers, which had
floated away on the waves:-).

It was a delightful hour. The waves glinted in the last rays of the sun, and
the marshland glowed in an unearthly light. Shadows fell everywhere,
suddenly transforming the sea-shore into another world- one full of strange
and beautiful entities. I for one fully expected Ravidasan to emerge out of
the sand dunes,:-)

And then, it was time to go. By 6.30 PM, a tired but exultant marched to the van, full of sea-water, shells and loads of Kodikarai sand-
only to be told sternly by our driver that he was not going to have his
clean van soiled by us in all our sand and filth. "enga venaa ukkarunga,
Mela venumnaa ukkarunga, anaa ulle gille vanthu asingam panneenga..."

It was at that moment, I think, that we all went crazy.:-)

[Later, we were all pretty sure that Poonghuzhali's 'aavi', floating around
the beaches of Kodikarai in spirit form, must have entered us all as
suitable to carry out her wishes- nothing else would explain what we
did.:-)]

One and all were hit by the same idea all at once. Kamal and Krupa darted
towards the rear, followed by Sathya, me, Anuradha, Lavanya, Radhika and
Viji- and before anyone knew what was happening, all of us had scrambled up
the slim ladder to the top of the carrier! It was a of course, with so many people- but that added to the general delight. All
the elders kept telling us that we were bound to fall off, that we'd have a
tough time making it to the vilage in one piece- and all their words fell on
deaf ears. We were determined on a 'crazy African Safari' (as Mr. Sharma put
it), and nothing would stop us.

The van driver gave up his eforts to get us down, and started his vehicle,
which gave a lurch and nearly upset some of us.:-)

And then started a weird and wonderful ride through the marshes of
Kodikarai- twilight had set in, the view from the top rolled away before our
eyes in all directions for miles, the sky was streaked with the blues and
pinks of sunset- in a word, it was superb.

The van went slowly, but there were the unavoidable humps and bumps in the
road, sending us all into yells whenever they happened. Krupashankar
proposed a contest to see who could stand up on the carrier, Lavanya was
seized by a desire to sing 'Alai kadalum oynthirukka..." (which she did:-),
and all of us were caught by the wonderful scenery. No deer were present,
but we suddenly came upon a lone and solitary...wild black horse, gambolling
in the flatlands! Of course, it must have been the horse ridden by the Oomai
Rani:-).

The original 'safari' was to have been until the gates of Kodikarai- but we
voted to ride on until the village was reached.

Sea-birds flew overheard, darkness was closing in, the lighthouse started
flashing (the only 'kolli-vai pisaasu' we could see) and the sea-wind
rippled across us. All around us, bushes and shrubs weaved along the road.
Some one from inside the van yelled to ask if we were all there on the roof,
and how many of us had been bumped off. The van obligingly gave another
lurch and set everyone screeching. All the animals and spirits must have
heard us for miles around:-)

Telephone wires began appearing, the mark of civlization- and then came the
agonizing question- we had climbed up easily enough, would climbing down be
that easy (especially Anuradha, -"...naan nadakkum pothE thoppu thoppunnu
vizhuven...":-)???

But we managed it. (see Krupa's photos:-). All the elders promptly got off
the van and stood around us, grinning and marking our 'progress' and
commenting wildly on how we must get down, where we must place our feet and
everything else. The locals gaped at this spectacular show.

Finally, all our dust left on the carrier, we settled down in our wet
clothes inside the van, playing 'Anthakshari' and generally fooling about.
Night had fallen and Kodikarai was left behind. Kudanthai was the night
halt, and when we reached it after three hours, and checked in Hotel
Chela's, all of us were ready for our rest.

(To be continued...)

Pavithra that was really well written. Kalakitte.
All,
We were fortunate to be accompanied by Mr.
Vishwaksenan, from whom we gathered lot of
information about sculptures, about temples, which
period they pertain to, how chalukya's influence
came about in later period sculptures, etc., etc.,
Thanks to Mr. Seetharaman, without whom we could not
have dreamt of covering all the places we had
planned to visit. Also Mr. Srinivasan & Mr.
Seetharama Sharma, who came with us had very good
historical knowledge that they shared with us.
When we were roaming around Periya Koil, Mr.
Vishwaksenan told us about how people in early days
used one kind of lamp to lighten the places. A
particular variety of Fish's eyes, Muthu (pearl),
Sea weed and some other thing were mixed together
(there is no detail as to how to mix them all, at
what temparature etc.,) and make the mixture into a
lamp shape. they then keep that lamp in sunlight for
few hrs and then tie it with a cloth. when taken out
at night, the thing will shine like a mercury lamp
through out the night. We know that people in
earlier days had lots of knowledge, but sad that
those discoveries were not properly documented.
What about ajantha paintings? they had drawn those
paintings more than 1,000 years ago and the
paintings still exist. What mooligais did they use?
Do anyone have any idea as to how they were able to
make those paintings. They have built great temples
and forts and maaligais. Can anyone even dream of
building similar structures now, even with all the
modern devices we have now. How would they have
taken those huge heavy weight stones to the top of
the structures? They were real genius.
We cannot rebuild a thanjavur koil or gangai konda
cholapuram or make such beautiful sculptures or
azhiyaa varnam konda paintings. But what we can do
is protect whatever exists and make people realize
the importance of our ancient cultural heritage.
I felt very sad seeing some of those sculptures and
paintings spoiled by some miscreants. Names had been
written over the paintings in the tanjore temple.
Those ignorant people probably thought that their
names are more important than those paintings.
In other places all those kalvettus were hidden
under cement surfaces. In the name of renovation
they had spoiled or hidden the important
masterpieces.
We all as a group should definitely strive to
preserve our heritage.
Regards,
Poonguzhali (Lavanya)
