MK's 'history' & Ganesha true story

V GANGADHARAN

The totally uncalled for but provocative observation by DMK
president and Chief Minister M Karunanidhi that Lord Ganesa was 'not a
native of Tamilnadu and that he was brought from Vatapi' has been
refuted by scholars and archaeologists.

Inaugurating the Anna Renaissance Scheme in Mamallapuram on 3
September, Karunanidhi with his typic acidic sarcasm said: 'This
Ganapathi is not a native of our place. He was born in Vatapi and was
brought here'.

Karunanidhi made this remark while dwelling about the origins
of the name of the town � Mamallapuram and the history of the Pallava
kingdom that ruled the region.

The DMK president said that Narasimhavarman also known as
Mamallan, a great Pallava king, defeated Pulikesi or Pulakesi at
Vatapi (7th century A.D.), the capital city of the Chalukyas (now
called Badami in Bijapur district in Karnataka) and his general
Paranjothi brought the image of Ganapathi or Vatapi Ganapathi to
Tamilnadu.

Interestingly, this is not the first time Karunanidhi has made
such an observation and this time round he has emphasised that this is
the true history.

Internationally renowned Epigraphist and Indologist Iravatham
Mahadevan commenting on the issue, however, said that the cult of
Ganesa was widespread in Tamilnadu even before Vatapi Ganapathi was
brought. The Pillayarpatti rock-cut cave temple near Karaikudi belongs
to the early Pandya period (6th Century A D) and the Pandya cave
temple is anterior to Narasimhavarman's time.

Mahadevan while tracing the antiquity of Ganesa said that an
excavation at Veerapuram in Kurnool district of Andhra Pradesh brought
to light a terracotta figurine of Ganesa datable to c. 50 B C to c.150
A D This suggests that the worship of Lord Ganapathi was popular in
Dravidian South India even in such early time.

He explained that in those days, the borders of Tamilnadu
extended upto Nellore and added that there was no reason to presume
that Ganesa worship was not prevalent in Tamilnadu prior to the
conquest of Vatapi by the Pallavas.

T Satyamurthy, former Superintending Archaeologist,
Archaeological Survey of India (representations of Ganesa or Pillayar were found well before Vatapi
Ganapathi image was brought to Tamilnadu. He further said that 'Vatapi
Ganapathi is only a war trophy' and referred to the Pillayarpatti
temple as evidence of Ganesa worship before Vatapi.

A rock-cut Ganesa, similar to that of Pillayarpatti is also
found in Kottukal in Kollam district of Kerala, datable to early
Pandya period. Explaining the recent excavation of Murugan temple
(belonging to 1st century A D) in Mamallapuram and the finding of
terracotta representation of Kuravai Koothu of Sangam literature
there, the former senior official of the excavations could unravel more about the worship of Ganesa in
Tamilnadu.

S. Ramachandran, formerly Epigraphist with the Archaeological
Department, Government of Tamilnadu, asserted that the worship of
Ganesa was prevalent in Tamilnadu much before Vatapi Ganesa and
Narasimhavarman's time. He referred to the work of M Arunachalam, a
renowned Tamil scholar who had published a research article (South
Indian Studies, Volume II, published by R Nagaswami, Society for
Archaeological, Historical and Epigraphical Research, 1978) on the
worship of Ganapathi in Tamilnadu during the Sangam age.

In that article, Arunachalam had taken up Purananuru verses
like Nallavam theeyavum alla to demonstrate the worship of Ganesa
during the ancient times. He also said that the period of Erukattur
Kon Peruntachan, the architect of Pillayarpatti temple as 520-550 AD,
which is well ahead of Narasimha Pallava.

T R Ganesan, Secretary, Saiva Tamizh Mandram, said that the
Tamil saint Avvaiyar (c. 5th century AD) had sung about the figure of
Ganesa in elephant form. Moreover, in Thirunavukarasar Tevaram, 4.2.5.
(Appar or Thirunavukarasar is a contemporary of Mahendravarman� I A D
580- 630 ) in the fourth thirumurai, second veerattanam, hymn 5, 'Pala
pala Kamatharagi......Ganapathi Enrum Kalirum' is a testimony to the
fact that the worship of Ganesa was prevalent in Tamilnadu before
Vatapi.

The Saiva Tamizh Mandram scholar, quoting 'Tamizhnattu
Kudaivarai Koilgal,' � a research book (published in December 2001) on
cave temples of Tamilnadu by eminent archaeologists, S Rajavelu and A
K Seshadri, (whose works have won the awards of the Government of
Tamilnadu) said that the authors of the book had categorically stated
that the sculptural representation of Ganesa in the caverns of the
Pandya period proves that the worship of Vinayaga or Ganesa was
widespread in Tamilnadu even before Vatapi Ganapathi was brought to
the Tamil Country as a war tropy.

Quoting from the same book, (page 2, para 4 and page 3, paras
1 and 2 and foreword by authors) TR Ganesan opined that the Hindu
temples in ancient Tamilnadu were built of perishable material like
brick, lime mortar and timber and most of these were lost to the fury
of nature.

He also felt that even if some of those brick temples had
survived, they would have proven that Ganesa images and its worship
preceded Pallava Narasimhavarman by at least several centuries and was
not 'imported' during his time, as 'presumed' by some.
