On Friday 10 Jul 2009 11:08:58 pm Sivapathasekaran wrote:
Godification is one such view-point..

Just compare the names of GOD with the KING - AANDAVAN - IRAIVAN etc...
they both are equated from early dictions themselves ...

Absolutely - we can see it right from the time of the Pharohs of Egypt, the
emperors of China and various monarchs of such lands, who were deified after
death, and literally worshipped.

A case could even be made for the deification and worship of various kings in
our own history and mythology; Rama and Krishna would be two obvious examples,
but what about Ayyappan, for example? They could all have been historical
kings who became deified.

There are several other view point too.

Democracy in Monarchy is something very rare .. found in TN .

Why in Uthiramerur DEMOCRACY inscriptions / details be given - by Great
grandfather of RRC - Parantaka I - when Monarchy had absolute Privelege TO
APPOINT HIS PERSONS - especially there was no puacity for HIS PERSONS when
he had such a Big Harem ?

This is actually what I wanted to share my thoughts about: It's a well known
fact that no matter how powerful or authoritarian a ruler, he can't rule
without at least the passive consent of the people he governs. If they don't
consent, there's a revolution, and another ruler comes to power. Or, there's
an invasion and his entire army defects to the other side. Or, in a democratic
system, he's simply voted out.

Various rulers over time have understood this: the Greeks of Athens, the
Romans (the Republic obviously, and unpopular emperors were dethroned quite
often in the history of the Roman empire), and so on come to mind. But what
about our own Mahajanapadas, Lichchavi, Videha, Kamboja and others? The
Arthashastra talks about the whole exercise of government being for the
benefit of the people, not the king.

In light of all this, I don't think it's remarkable that the Cholas (and
Cheras and Pandyas and Pallavas, probably) should have instituted a system of
local democratic governance, with some amount of controls, to make sure that
the people remained happy and consented to their rule.

What _is_ interesting to me is how elaborate the "constitution" of the
Uttiramerur inscription is - it's not merely a "katta panchayat", it's a
pretty well thought out body of rules and procedures. I'm sure this evolved
over time, and had quite a bit of change over the years.

Unfortunately, we only have snapshots of history...
